

# **The Society and the Organism: Re-evaluating Minsky and Varela in the Age of Generative and Embodied AI**

## **Part I: The Mind as a Society of Agents \- Marvin Minsky's Computational Architecture**

At the heart of 20th-century attempts to demystify human intelligence lies a profound divergence in philosophical and technical approaches. One of the most influential and enduring conceptual frameworks comes from Marvin Minsky, a founding father of artificial intelligence. In his seminal 1986 book, *The Society of Mind*, Minsky articulated a vision of intelligence that was radically modular and computational.1 Rejecting the notion of a unified, monolithic "self" or a single, perfect principle governing thought, he proposed that the mind is best understood as a vast, bustling society. This society is composed of a multitude of simple, specialized, and individually mindless processes he termed "agents." For Minsky, the magic of intelligence, consciousness, and even emotion is not a property of any single component but an emergent phenomenon arising from the managed, and often chaotic, interactions within this internal society.1 His core philosophy, encapsulated in the tenet that "minds are what brains do," posits that the power of human cognition stems not from a singular, elegant law, but from immense diversity.1 This section will explore the core tenets of Minsky's theory, dissect its key architectural components, and examine the vision for artificial intelligence it inspired—a vision that, after decades of appearing more philosophical than practical, is finding startling new relevance in the architectures of modern AI.

### **1.1. The Core Tenets of 'The Society of Mind'**

Minsky's central thesis is that intelligence is constructed from the collaboration of simpler parts. The mind, in his view, is a "society of mind," an intricate organization of small processes called agents that work in concert to produce complex phenomena like action, thought, commonsense reasoning, and memory.3 This model is fundamentally decentralized. In contrast to traditional views of the mind as a central processor, Minsky's model has no "master" agent in control.5 Instead, intelligence emerges from the complex interplay of agents that collaborate, compete, and even conflict with one another.5 Some agents are dedicated to processing sensory information, others to motor functions, and still others to abstract thought or managing emotional states.3 This decentralized, non-hierarchical structure is not a flaw but a feature, providing the mind with its remarkable versatility, resilience, and adaptability through duplication and the availability of alternative perspectives.3

The theory dignifies the notion that the mind is composed of a great diversity of mechanisms, each unique to the individual yet part of a multifaceted society of structures and processes.6 These agents are highly specialized, unlike the general-purpose nature of a complete human being.6 One agent might be responsible for a granular task like distinguishing the color red, while a higher-level agency coordinates the complex processes of language.5 Even our sense of self, consciousness, and our experiences of pain and pleasure are, in this framework, not fundamental entities but are themselves constructed from the interactions of simpler, mindless agents.3 Pain and pleasure, for instance, function as powerful agents that help the mind prioritize which procedures to execute.3

Crucially, Minsky's theory was not born from abstract philosophical speculation alone. It was deeply rooted in his pioneering work in robotics and artificial intelligence at the MIT AI Laboratory, which he co-founded.7 He stated that the biggest source of ideas for the theory came from his practical attempts in the early 1970s to create a machine that could build with children's blocks using a robotic arm, a video camera, and a computer.1 This origin story is revealing; it shows that the Society of Mind is fundamentally a theory about

*how to get things done*. It is an engineer's perspective on intelligence, focused on functional decomposition and the problem of orchestrating a multitude of simple processes to achieve a complex goal. The challenge of teaching a robot to see a block, grasp it, and place it on another block forced Minsky to think about how different skills—vision, motor control, planning, goal management—must be broken down into smaller sub-problems and then coordinated. This practical, problem-solving-oriented foundation distinguishes his work from purely philosophical or neuroscientific accounts of the mind.

### **1.2. The Architectural Components: Agents, K-Lines, and Frames**

To give structure to his theory, Minsky introduced a novel vocabulary to describe the functional components of his mental society. These concepts provide a blueprint for how a thinking machine might be organized.

**Agents:** The foundational units of the Society of Mind are agents. An agent is a simple process, comparable in scale to a subroutine or data structure in a computer program.6 By themselves, agents are mindless and possess no significant intelligence.1 For example, a "Builder" agent that assembles blocks does not understand building; it merely activates a sequence of other, simpler agents like "Find-block," "Get-block," and "Put-block".3 Intelligence arises only when these agents are connected and composed into larger systems or "societies of agents" that can perform functions more complex than any single agent could.6 This architecture allows for the construction of higher-level mental phenomena. The sense of self, for example, is not a central executive but a complex society of agents built upon other agents that manage goals, memories, and self-models.3

**K-Lines (Knowledge-Lines):** Minsky's theory of memory deviates from the simple storage-and-retrieval model. He proposed "K-lines" as a mechanism for recreating past mental states.5 A K-line is not a memory of a fact, but a wire-like connection that attaches to all the agents that were active during a particular problem-solving attempt or mental experience.5 When you later face a similar problem, activating that K-line simultaneously turns on the same set of agents, effectively putting your mind back into a state that was previously useful.3 This explains how we retrieve not just data, but entire problem-solving strategies. The theory proposes that "we keep each thing we learn close to the agents that learn it in the first place".3 K-lines can connect to other K-lines, forming vast societies of knowledge that are organized into "level-bands," allowing the mind to focus on granular details and large-scale plans simultaneously.3

**Frames:** To address the challenge of commonsense reasoning and language understanding, Minsky introduced the concept of "frames" in a seminal 1974 paper that predated his book.3 A frame is a skeletal data structure, a template for representing a stereotyped situation, like being in a child's birthday party or a living room.3 This structure contains "slots" that can be filled with specific details. For instance, a "living room" frame would have slots for a couch, chairs, and windows. Crucially, these slots come with "default assumptions"—a basic idea of what to expect (e.g., a couch is for sitting on) that can be modified as more specific information is gained.3 Frames provide context, allowing us to make inferences and understand stories and conversations. Most human communication is possible because we share common frames of reference, allowing us to fill in the vast unstated details in everyday language.2

Together, these components—managed through a system of parallel processing where multiple agents work simultaneously 5 and are governed by other agents like "censors" and "suppressors" to resolve conflicts and set priorities 11—form a comprehensive, conceptual architecture for intelligence.

### **1.3. Minsky's Vision for Artificial Intelligence**

Minsky was not merely a theorist; he was a fervent and determined advocate for the field of Artificial Intelligence.7 He championed the idea that computers would one day not only think like humans but eventually surpass us, and he had little patience for those who doubted this possibility.7 His life's work was dedicated to creating machines that could replicate human cognitive processes—learning, reasoning, and problem-solving—in the belief that this was the key to unlocking truly intelligent systems that could benefit society.12

His approach was fundamentally computational and top-down. He viewed the brain as a machine whose functions could be studied and replicated in a computer, which would in turn teach us more about our own minds.8 This is evident in his focus on knowledge representation. The "Frames" paper created a new paradigm in this area, becoming a cornerstone of what would later be called "Good Old-Fashioned AI" (GOFAI), which emphasizes symbolic reasoning and explicit knowledge structures.9 Minsky believed that a primary challenge for AI was endowing machines with the vast repository of "common sense" that humans use effortlessly.8

It is important to recognize that *The Society of Mind* was not written as a scientific textbook to prove a specific hypothesis about AI or to map directly onto physical brain structures.1 Minsky was frustrated with what he saw as the limitations of both neuroscience and psychology. He was critical of neuroscientists who focused exclusively on a bottom-up study of individual neurons, quipping, "Can you reverse engineer a Pentium chip by thinking at the level of transistors?".13 He argued for the necessity of many high-level, top-down theories to guide experimentation. At the same time, he was wary of the "physics envy" in psychology—the attempt to explain the mind with a handful of simple, elegant laws. The brain, he argued, is a messy product of millions of years of evolution, and understanding it would require many rules, not a few.13 Consequently,

*The Society of Mind* is a collection of ideas, a conceptual exploration of how mind and thinking might work, intended to inspire and organize further thought rather than to present a finished, empirically verified model.1

This philosophical and conceptual nature led many to dismiss the theory as an interesting but impractical relic, especially as the field of AI shifted away from symbolic approaches and toward the seemingly monolithic architectures of deep learning.14 In the 1990s and 2000s, Minsky's vision of a society of interacting symbolic agents appeared far removed from the sleek, end-to-end trained neural networks that were gaining prominence. However, this dismissal was premature. The very limitations of those monolithic models have forced the field to rediscover the wisdom in Minsky's architectural vision. His ideas were not wrong; they were merely waiting for the computational and algorithmic tools capable of realizing them.

This has become strikingly apparent with the evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs). The initial success of models like GPT-3 was based on scaling up a single, massive neural network. Yet, as these models grew, they encountered prohibitive computational costs and issues with generalization. The solution that has emerged is a pivot back toward modularity. Modern architectures like Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models are, in effect, a direct operationalization of Minsky's theory.14 In an MoE model, an input is not processed by the entire network. Instead, a "router" network—itself a small neural net—selects a few specialized "expert" sub-networks to handle the task. These experts are Minsky's "agents," and the router is the managing agency that decides which part of the "society" is best suited for the current problem.15

This trend extends beyond MoE to the burgeoning field of multi-agent AI systems. Frameworks like AutoGPT explicitly embody the Society of Mind principle of functional decomposition.15 When given a high-level objective, AutoGPT breaks it down into smaller, concrete steps. It then employs distinct agentic components—a planner, a memory manager, and an executor that can browse the web or run code—that work together to achieve the goal.15 This is a clear, modern implementation of a society of specialized agents collaborating on a task. Minsky's work was not just a philosophical treatise; it was an architectural blueprint decades ahead of its time, providing a powerful and prescient conceptual framework for designing the scalable, robust, and modular AI systems that are now at the cutting edge of the field.

Furthermore, a deeper look into Minsky's intellectual history reveals a connection that bridges the supposed gap between his symbolic approach and the learning-centric paradigms of today. While he became famous for his work on symbolic systems like Frames and for his critique of early, simplistic neural networks called perceptrons 9, his academic origins lie elsewhere. His 1954 doctoral dissertation was titled "Theory of Neural-Analog Reinforcement Systems and Its Application to the Brain-Model Problem".9 In 1951, he built the SNARC (Stochastic Neural Analog Reinforcement Calculator), one of the very first neural network learning machines, which used trial-and-error reinforcement to adjust its own synaptic weights.9

This early work in reinforcement learning (RL) is not a historical footnote but a key to a more nuanced understanding of his later theories. It suggests that Minsky was never dogmatically anti-learning or anti-connectionist. His critique of perceptrons was a mathematical analysis of their specific limitations, not a rejection of learning itself. His later focus on the *organization* of intelligence in *The Society of Mind* can therefore be re-interpreted. The theory is not necessarily about a society of *symbolic* agents. It is a high-level organizational principle for managing a diversity of problem-solving methods, whatever their nature.

From this perspective, the "agents" in the society need not be hard-coded subroutines. They can be viewed as individual RL agents, each having learned its own specialized "policy"—a strategy for acting based on environmental feedback.17 The "society" becomes the overarching architecture that orchestrates these competing and cooperating policies, deciding which agent's learned heuristics are best suited for a given context. This reframes Minsky's theory from a GOFAI relic into a highly relevant framework for modern multi-agent RL and the AI alignment problem. The challenge of alignment—ensuring that an AI system's behavior conforms to human goals—is fundamentally a problem of managing the emergent behavior of many interacting, goal-seeking subsystems.20 Minsky's work provides a rich conceptual toolkit for thinking about how to structure this internal governance, bridging the historical divide between symbolic AI and connectionism and demonstrating its enduring relevance to the most advanced frontiers of AI research.

## **Part II: The Mind as Embodied Enactment \- Francisco Varela's Biological Grounding**

In stark contrast to Minsky's computational and top-down architectural view, the work of Chilean neuroscientist and philosopher Francisco Varela offers a radical, bottom-up alternative. Developed with colleagues like Humberto Maturana and Evan Thompson, Varela's philosophy, known as **enactivism**, rejects the very foundations of the cognitivist tradition that Minsky represents. For Varela, the mind is not a computer program running on brain hardware, nor is it a society of abstract agents residing "in the head".21 Instead, cognition is an activity that is fundamentally inseparable from life itself—a process of "sense-making" that is deeply

**embodied**, **embedded** in an environment, and **enacted** through the dynamic interaction between a living organism and its world.22 This perspective grounds the study of mind not in logic or computation, but in biology, phenomenology, and dynamical systems theory. It argues that to understand the mind, one must first understand the precarious, self-sustaining nature of a living being. This section will delve into the core principles of Varela's enactive approach, explore its radical rejection of mental representation, analyze the significant criticisms leveled against it, and articulate the distinct vision for AI that emerges from this biological grounding.

### **2.1. The Enactive Turn: Core Principles of Embodied Cognition**

The enactive approach is often summarized by the "4E" framework, which posits that cognition is **Embodied**, **Embedded**, **Extended**, and **Enacted**.24 This framework represents a fundamental departure from the traditional brain-centric view of the mind.

* **Embodied:** Cognition is not localized in the brain but depends directly on the entire body as a functional whole.21 The body's sensorimotor capabilities—how it moves, senses, and interacts with the world—are not mere inputs and outputs for a central cognitive processor; they are constitutive of cognition itself.23 Our bodily experiences fundamentally shape our perception, memory, and decision-making.23  
* **Embedded:** Cognitive processes are inextricably shaped by the environment in which the organism is situated.22 The environment is not a passive backdrop for cognition but an active participant in the cognitive process.22 Context is not just additional information; it is part of the cognitive system itself.  
* **Extended:** This principle, most famously articulated by Clark and Chalmers, suggests that cognitive processes can literally extend beyond the skin, incorporating external tools and artifacts.25 A person using a notebook to remember information is not just aiding their internal memory; the notebook becomes part of the cognitive system performing the act of remembering.  
* **Enacted:** This is perhaps the most central and radical concept. Enactivism argues that cognition is not about representing a pre-given, independent world. Instead, it is the "enactment" or "bringing forth" of a world of significance through embodied action.22 The world an organism experiences is not an objective reality that is passively received but a domain of meaning that co-emerges from the organism's history of interactions.25

At the core of this framework is the idea of cognition as **sense-making**. For an organism, to live is to exist in a precarious state, constantly acting to maintain its own integrity.24 Sense-making is this fundamental activity of navigating the environment to sustain its existence. This process is inherently evaluative; the world is perceived in terms of what is relevant, beneficial, or harmful to the organism's continued being.25 This is the very beginning of mind and value.24

This view necessitates the inseparability of perception and action. Drawing from the phenomenological tradition of Maurice Merleau-Ponty, enactivism posits that we do not first perceive the world and then decide how to act. Rather, perception and action are two sides of the same coin, reciprocally influencing one another.23 We perceive the world in terms of the actions it affords us. The experience of seeing an object is not just about the light hitting our retina; it is deeply connected to how our sensory experience changes as we move our eyes, head, and body in relation to that object.23 Varela formalized this through the concept of

**sensorimotor contingencies**: the lawful, predictable relationships between our motor actions and the resulting sensory changes.22 Mastering these contingencies

*is* perceiving. In this view, seeing is not an event that happens inside the brain; it is an activity that unfolds in the dynamic, interactive relationship between the embodied agent and its world.24

### **2.2. Autopoiesis and the Radical Rejection of Representationalism**

The biological bedrock of Varela's enactivism is the concept of **autopoiesis**, or "self-creation," which he developed with his mentor Humberto Maturana.22 Autopoiesis describes the fundamental organization of the living. An autopoietic system, such as a single cell, is defined as a network of processes that recursively produces and maintains its own components, including the boundary (e.g., the cell membrane) that separates it from its environment.24 This "operational closure" means the system's identity is defined by its own self-sustaining activities, granting it autonomy from its surroundings.25 The emergence of this boundary creates a fundamental distinction between self and non-self, giving rise to a primitive identity.25

From this biological principle, Varela derives the **life-mind continuity thesis**: the organizational principles of life are the same as the organizational principles of mind.24 Cognition is not something that was added to life at a later stage of evolution. Rather, the process of living—of an autopoietic system actively maintaining itself in a precarious world—is already a cognitive process. It is sense-making.24 The nervous system, in this view, does not

*create* mind from non-mental matter. It serves to *expand* the range and complexity of the sense-making that is already inherent in life, allowing for the coordination of a multicellular body that moves over large distances.24

This biological foundation leads directly to enactivism's most profound and controversial claim: its radical rejection of **representationalism**. The dominant paradigm in cognitive science, and the one implicitly underlying Minsky's work, is that the mind functions by creating and manipulating internal representations—symbols, maps, or models—of an external world that is assumed to exist independently of the observer.21 Varela and his colleagues argued that this entire picture is fundamentally flawed.25

If an organism's world is "enacted" or "brought forth" through its history of interactions, then there is no pre-given, independent world for the mind to represent.26 The organism and its environment are not two separate entities that later come into contact; they are "structurally coupled," co-emerging and co-evolving through a shared history of interaction.25 The path is not pre-laid; it is created in the walking. Therefore, the core business of cognition cannot be to build an accurate internal model of an external reality. Instead, cognition is the very process of enacting a meaningful world through embodied, sense-making activity. This is not a mere tweak to cognitivism; it is a call to abandon its core tenets and its familiar explanatory strategies, such as the input-processing-output model of the mind.26

### **2.3. Criticisms and Counterarguments to the Enactive Framework**

Varela's radical reconceptualization of mind has faced significant criticism from philosophers and scientists who find its claims to be overstated, philosophically costly, and difficult to operationalize.

One of the most pointed critiques comes from the philosopher Daniel Dennett. While sympathetic to some of the enactivists' points, Dennett argues that their call for a revolution in cognitive science is unwarranted, suggesting it is more of a "modest revision of emphases".31 The core of his disagreement lies with the rejection of a "pre-given" environment. Drawing on the work of biologist Richard Lewontin, Varela argues that organisms actively constitute their own environments.31 Dennett concedes that this is true on an evolutionary timescale—the oxygen in our atmosphere is a product of life—but argues that for the purposes of ongoing cognitive research, the environment is largely stable and can be safely treated as an external, pre-given condition.31 The color of a tree does not change as an animal perceives it. Dennett challenges the enactivists to show the practical, hands-on applications of their view in cognitive science. He remains unconvinced that complex cognition is possible without internal states that function as representations. In his memorable phrase, for any sufficiently complex cognitive system, "something

*awfully like* representation is going to *have* to creep in like the tide".31

A second line of criticism focuses on the philosophical foundations of enactivism, arguing that it rests on "very costly ontological premises" that are not universally accepted in the scientific community.32 The claim that autopoiesis, an organizational property, is the source of meaning and sense-making is a profound leap that many find unsubstantiated.32 Critics also question the necessity of the body's role being

*constitutive* of cognition, rather than simply being a crucial *causal* factor. The theory's reliance on phenomenological insights and its rejection of falsifiable models lead some to view it as more a work of philosophy than a scientific research program.33 Furthermore, critics point out that existing AI systems are not autopoietic, and that higher forms of cognition, such as abstract thought, have not been convincingly explained by enactivist principles.32

A third, more subtle critique comes from within the enactivist tradition itself, articulated by Varela's own co-author, Evan Thompson. This concerns the "looping effects" of studying human consciousness, particularly contemplative practices like mindfulness meditation, which were central to Varela's project of neurophenomenology.21 Drawing on the philosopher Ian Hacking, Thompson points out that when scientists define and measure a human category like "mindfulness," they create a new way for people to understand and experience themselves. Meditators begin to interpret their own inner lives through the lens of the scientific concepts used to study them. This creates a feedback loop: the object of study (the practice of mindfulness) is changed by the very act of studying it.21 This "making up people" phenomenon complicates any claim to be observing the mind as it "really is," and it highlights how scientific inquiry is itself an interactive, world-shaping activity, a point consistent with enactivism but one that poses a deep methodological challenge to it.

### **2.4. Varela's Vision for Artificial Intelligence**

The implications of enactivism for artificial intelligence are as radical as its theory of mind. Varela's work suggests that the entire project of traditional, disembodied AI is misguided. If cognition arises from the dynamic interplay between an embodied agent and its environment, then true intelligence cannot be programmed into a disembodied, abstract system.22

Varela's vision for AI points toward two intertwined research programs: **embodied robotics** and **artificial life**. The path to creating truly intelligent systems is not through bigger datasets or faster processors alone, but through building machines that are embodied and embedded in a physical environment.22 The focus shifts from developing algorithms that process information to designing adaptive and flexible robots that can learn from their interactions with the world.29 The concept of autopoiesis further suggests that the ultimate goal is to create artificial life—systems that are autonomous and self-maintaining.26 In this view, intelligence would not be a pre-programmed feature but an emergent property of the system's struggle to maintain its own existence in a complex, dynamic environment. This stands in direct opposition to Minsky's vision of engineering intelligence by composing functional, computational agents. For Varela, intelligence is not engineered; it is grown through a history of embodied interaction.

While the "looping effect" critique was originally aimed at the study of human contemplative practices, it can be powerfully generalized to the entire endeavor of creating and living with AI. If Varela's core tenet holds—that our minds are not fixed entities but are continuously enacted through our interactions with our world—then the introduction of powerful AI systems into that world is not a neutral event. These systems are becoming a significant and pervasive part of our environment. Our daily interactions with AI companions, generative content tools, and automated assistants are precisely the kinds of structural couplings that, according to enactivism, must reshape our own cognitive processes. We offload mental tasks, develop new hybrid modes of thinking in collaboration with AI, and our very self-concept begins to shift in response to these new intelligent others. This reframes the AI alignment problem. The danger is not merely the instrumental risk of what a misaligned superintelligence might *do* to us as a separate object. The deeper, more Varelian risk is the transformative effect on human cognition itself—a co-evolutionary process where the creator and the creation are locked in a feedback loop, mutually shaping one another in ways we are only beginning to comprehend. The development of AGI is not a one-way street; it is a participatory sense-making process on a global scale.

Similarly, Dennett's pragmatic critique, while intended to temper the revolutionary claims of enactivism, inadvertently serves as a bridge between the two seemingly irreconcilable worlds of Minsky and Varela. Dennett's insistence that something "awfully like representation" is necessary for complex cognition does not fully reject the importance of embodiment; it calls for a pragmatic middle ground.31 This position highlights the limitations of a "strong" or purely reactive enactivism, which struggles to account for long-term planning and abstract thought. At the same time, the history of GOFAI demonstrates the brittleness of purely symbolic, ungrounded systems. Dennett's critique, therefore, does not close the door on Varela's project but instead points toward the very synthesis that defines the frontier of modern AI research: the creation of hybrid models. These are systems that are fundamentally embodied and learn through interaction, as Varela would demand, but which use this interaction to build, refine, and exploit internal models or representations of their world, as Minsky and Dennett would find necessary. The future of AI may not belong to Minsky or Varela alone, but to the Varelian agent that learns to build a Minskyan mind.

## **Part III: The State of the Art \- Dominant Paradigms in Contemporary AI**

To evaluate the legacies of Minsky and Varela, it is essential to first establish a clear understanding of the dominant technological paradigms shaping the current landscape of artificial intelligence. The field is advancing along several key fronts, each with distinct architectures, capabilities, and philosophical implications. This section will provide a technically robust but accessible overview of three pivotal areas: the rise of Generative AI and Large Language Models (LLMs), which have captured the public imagination; the interactive paradigm of Reinforcement Learning (RL), which powers autonomous decision-making; and the ambitious frontier of Embodied AGI, which seeks to ground intelligence in the physical world. These technologies serve as the empirical data against which the theories of Minsky and Varela will be measured.

### **3.1. The Age of Generative AI and Large Language Models (LLMs)**

The most visible AI trend of the 2020s has been the explosion of Generative AI, with Large Language Models (LLMs) like OpenAI's GPT series, Google's Gemini, and Anthropic's Claude at its forefront.35 These systems are a category of deep learning models capable of generating novel content—including text, images, and software code—that mimics the data on which they were trained.36

The technical foundation for most modern LLMs is the **transformer architecture**, first introduced in a 2017 paper.39 Unlike previous neural networks that processed text sequentially, the transformer uses a mechanism called

**self-attention** to weigh the importance of different words in an input sequence simultaneously, allowing it to capture complex, long-range dependencies and context.40 This parallel processing capability made it feasible to train models on an unprecedented scale.40 The training process involves feeding the model a massive corpus of text, often scraped from large portions of the internet, and teaching it a simple objective: predict the next word in a sequence.41 Through this self-supervised process, the model learns the statistical patterns of grammar, semantics, facts, and reasoning styles embedded within its training data, encoding this "knowledge" into billions or even trillions of numerical parameters.40

By their very nature, LLMs are fundamentally **disembodied**. They learn not from direct, interactive experience with the world, but from a static, textual representation of it.26 Their understanding is not grounded in sensory perception or physical action but is derived entirely from the statistical relationships between tokens in their training data. This leads to both their remarkable capabilities and their characteristic failure modes. LLMs excel at tasks that rely on manipulating learned linguistic patterns: they can generate fluent and coherent text, summarize complex documents, translate languages, and write code with impressive proficiency.39 However, their disembodied nature makes them prone to "hallucinations" or "confabulations," where they generate plausible-sounding but factually incorrect information.46 Because their knowledge is not grounded in physical reality, they often fail at tasks requiring basic commonsense reasoning about the world, such as understanding object permanence or the physical consequences of actions.47

### **3.2. The Interactive Paradigm: Reinforcement Learning (RL)**

While Generative AI learns from static datasets, Reinforcement Learning (RL) represents a paradigm of learning through interaction. RL is a machine learning technique in which an autonomous **agent** learns to make decisions by performing **actions** within an **environment** to maximize a cumulative **reward** signal.17 It is a formalized process of trial-and-error, inspired by behavioral psychology, that allows an agent to discover an optimal strategy, or

**policy**, for achieving a goal without explicit instruction or labeled data.17

The core of RL is the agent-environment loop. At each step, the agent observes the current **state** of the environment, chooses an action based on its current policy, and receives a reward (or penalty) and the new state of the environment as feedback.17 The agent's goal is to learn a policy that maximizes the total expected reward over the long term. This process involves navigating the critical

**exploration-exploitation trade-off**: the agent must decide whether to exploit actions that have yielded high rewards in the past or to explore new, untried actions that might lead to even better outcomes.18

RL is inherently suited for sequential decision-making problems in dynamic and uncertain environments.17 It is the primary method used to train AI to master complex games like Chess and Go (e.g., AlphaZero) and is a key technology for robotics, autonomous vehicle control, and resource optimization systems.18 Unlike supervised learning, which learns a static mapping from input to output, RL is about learning to

*act* over time to achieve a goal.

### **3.3. The Frontier of Embodied AGI**

The limitations of disembodied models like LLMs and the interactive power of RL have converged to fuel intense interest in **Embodied AI**. This field aims to give AI systems a physical body—such as a robot—enabling them to perceive, act, and learn through real-time interaction with the physical world.50 Many researchers view embodiment not just as another application area for AI, but as a crucial, perhaps essential, pathway toward achieving Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)—an AI with human-level capabilities across a wide range of tasks.48

The central challenge of Embodied AI is to bridge the "reality gap"—the immense gulf between the clean, structured data of digital environments and the messy, unpredictable, and dynamic nature of the physical world.52 This involves solving formidable technical problems, including the integration of multiple sensory modalities (vision, touch, hearing), real-time adaptation to changing conditions, and learning from the continuous, highly correlated streams of data generated by physical interaction.52 Safety is also a paramount concern, as mistakes made by a physical agent have real-world consequences.52

To structure the pursuit of this ambitious goal, researchers have proposed developmental roadmaps that categorize Embodied AGI into progressive levels of capability. While specific frameworks vary, they generally follow a trajectory from narrow competence to full generality, providing a clear metric for assessing progress and understanding the challenges that lie ahead.53

## **Part IV: A Tale of Two Alignments \- Mapping Modern AI to Minsky and Varela**

With the theoretical frameworks of Minsky and Varela established and the dominant paradigms of contemporary AI outlined, the stage is set for a comparative analysis. This section will dissect the state-of-the-art in AI through the lenses of these two pioneering thinkers. The investigation reveals a fascinating and deeply consequential paradox: the most powerful AI systems of the current era are being built with architectures that echo Minsky's "society of mind," yet their fundamental nature as disembodied, text-trained entities represents the philosophical antithesis of Varela's enactivism. The entire frontier of embodied AI can be understood as a grand attempt to resolve this tension by placing Minskyan-style minds into Varelian bodies, charting a course toward a future that is neither purely computational nor purely reactive, but a necessary synthesis of both.

### **4.1. Echoes of Minsky: The Society of Agents in Modern Architectures**

For decades, Minsky's *The Society of Mind* was often regarded as a brilliant but largely metaphorical work, its vision of a vast, decentralized collective of agents being too complex to implement with the technology of the time.11 The rise of monolithic deep learning models seemed to push his ideas further into the realm of historical curiosity.14 However, as these monolithic models have begun to hit scaling limits, the field has, in a remarkable turn, converged on architectures that are direct, functional implementations of Minsky's theory.

The most prominent example is the **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** architecture, now used in leading LLMs like Mistral's Mixtral and Google's Gemini.14 In a standard LLM, every input query activates the entire network's billions of parameters, a computationally expensive process. An MoE model, by contrast, consists of a "router" network and a set of smaller, specialized "expert" networks. The router analyzes the input and selectively activates only a few relevant experts to process it. These experts are, for all practical purposes, Minsky's "agents"—specialized subsystems trained for different types of tasks (e.g., one expert might be better at coding, another at poetry). The router is the managing agency that orchestrates the society. This modular design is a direct instantiation of Minsky's core idea: intelligence emerges from the collaboration of diverse, specialized components.15

This Minskyan alignment is even more explicit in the burgeoning field of **multi-agent systems**. Frameworks such as AutoGPT and agentic workflows using tools like LangChain are built on the principle of functional decomposition.15 A high-level goal provided by a user is broken down into a series of sub-tasks, each of which is assigned to a specialized agent. One agent might be a "planner," another a "web searcher," a third a "code executor," and a fourth a "memory manager".15 The final output is a product of their coordinated interaction. This mirrors Minsky's description of a "Builder" agent that accomplishes its goal by activating a sequence of simpler agents.3

Furthermore, advanced techniques for improving AI reasoning are explicitly creating miniature "societies" that leverage internal debate and critique. Actor-critic models, where one agent (the "actor") proposes an action or solution and a second agent (the "critic") evaluates it, are a staple of reinforcement learning and are now being applied to LLMs.15 This dynamic of proposal and revision, of collaboration and conflict between agents, is precisely the mechanism Minsky argued gives intelligence its robustness and versatility.3 Finally, the trend toward

**neuro-symbolic integration**—augmenting connectionist LLMs with symbolic tools like calculators, databases, and code interpreters—reflects Minsky's belief in a hybrid mind composed of different kinds of agents. The LLM acts as a high-level executive, a "glue" that coordinates a society of specialized tools, some neural and some symbolic, to solve a problem.15 In architecture and function, the most advanced AI systems of today are unmistakably Minskyan.

### **4.2. The Varelian Trajectory: Embodiment as the Next Frontier**

While the internal architecture of modern AI aligns with Minsky, its developmental trajectory is being driven by a profound recognition of Varela's central thesis: disembodied intelligence is fundamentally limited. The very failures of LLMs—their lack of grounding, their inability to reason about the physical world, their nonsensical hallucinations—have catalyzed a massive research effort to connect them to physical reality, an implicit acknowledgment that knowledge derived solely from text is brittle and incomplete.47 This push toward embodiment represents a powerful, industry-wide Varelian turn.

The primary vehicle for this turn is **embodied reinforcement learning**. When an agent is trained via RL within an interactive simulator or a real-world robot, it is engaging in a process that is quintessentially Varelian.56 The agent is not being fed a static dataset; it is learning by

*doing*. It acts, senses the consequences of its actions, and updates its behavior accordingly. Through this history of sensorimotor interaction, it "enacts" a world of meaningful states and actions relevant to its goals. This process is inherently embodied, interactive, and grounded in a feedback loop with an environment—the core principles of enactivism.

The evolution from disembodied LLMs to embodied agents like **LLaRP (Large Language model Reinforcement Learning Policy)** perfectly illustrates this Varelian trajectory.62 LLaRP takes a pre-trained, disembodied LLM and explicitly adapts it to serve as the control policy for a

*visual, embodied* agent. The system learns to "see and act" by using RL to connect the LLM's abstract linguistic capabilities to concrete visual inputs and motor outputs within an interactive environment. This is a deliberate attempt to cure the LLM of its disembodied nature by forcing it to ground its knowledge through sensorimotor experience. The entire field of embodied AI can be seen as the engineering equivalent of Varela's philosophical project: the attempt to build minds that arise from the coupling of a body, its actions, and its environment.

### **4.3. The Central Paradox: Disembodied Minds with Social Structures**

The simultaneous alignment of AI with these two opposing philosophies creates the central paradox of our current technological moment. The most powerful and influential AI systems, namely LLMs and their derivatives, are being constructed with an internal architecture—a society of agents—that Minsky envisioned. Yet their fundamental substrate—a disembodied, non-interactive, purely representational model trained on a static snapshot of text—is the exact cognitivist paradigm that Varela's enactivism was formulated to reject.

We are building minds that are structured like Minsky's societies but are populated by agents that have never experienced the world Varela deemed essential for meaning. This explains their paradoxical nature: they possess an astonishing, superhuman fluency with the syntactic and statistical patterns of language, yet they exhibit a sub-human fragility in semantics and commonsense understanding. They can write a sonnet in the style of Shakespeare about the experience of tasting a strawberry, but they do not know what a strawberry is, what tasting is, or that dropping one will cause it to fall. The "society" is a committee of brilliant savants who have memorized every book in the library but have never set foot outside the building. Their knowledge is vast but ungrounded, a mile wide and an inch deep.

This paradox is not sustainable. The limitations of this ungrounded social intelligence are now the primary drivers of AI research. The resolution, as evidenced by the Varelian trajectory of the field, is the project of embodiment. The frontier of AI is the attempt to resolve this paradox by placing the Minskyan "brain" into a Varelian "body," forcing the society of agents to ground its abstract, symbolic, and statistical knowledge through direct, physical interaction with the world.

The future of AI is therefore neither purely Minskyan nor purely Varelian; it is necessarily a hybrid. The history of AI has demonstrated the limitations of each approach in isolation. The purely symbolic, disembodied systems of GOFAI, which align with a narrow reading of Minsky, proved too brittle and unable to cope with the complexity of the real world. Conversely, purely reactive, non-representational robotic systems, which align with a naive reading of Varela, struggle to scale to tasks requiring long-term planning and abstract reasoning.

The limitations of today's LLMs—the peak of disembodied, representation-based systems—are now forcing a synthesis. The solution emerging from the research literature is to *use* the powerful representational and reasoning capabilities of LLM-like agents but to *constrain, ground, and validate* them through Varelian embodiment and interactive learning. The debate between Minsky and Varela should not be seen as a zero-sum game to be won by one side. Rather, they should be viewed as providing the two essential, complementary halves of a complete theory of intelligence: Minsky provides the blueprint for the internal **architecture**, while Varela provides the necessary conditions for the external **grounding**. The most promising path to AGI lies in their synthesis.

## **Part V: Conclusion and Recommendations \- Charting the Path Forward**

The parallel explorations of Marvin Minsky's computational society and Francisco Varela's embodied organism reveal more than just a historical debate in the philosophy of mind; they provide an essential diagnostic framework for understanding the state of artificial intelligence today and a normative guide for its future. The current AI landscape is defined by a central paradox born from the partial adoption of both visions. The path to robust, general, and aligned AI lies not in choosing one pioneer over the other, but in embracing a deliberate and principled synthesis of their complementary insights.

### **5.1. Synthesis: A Hybrid Future**

The analysis of contemporary AI trends makes one conclusion inescapable: the future of AI is hybrid. The most powerful AI systems, particularly Large Language Models, are increasingly built on Minskyan architectural principles. The shift from monolithic models to modular Mixture-of-Experts and multi-agent systems is a tacit admission that intelligence at scale requires a "society" of diverse, specialized components collaborating to solve problems. Minsky's vision of a decentralized, functional architecture has proven to be not a philosophical metaphor but a practical engineering roadmap for building scalable AI.

Simultaneously, the entire field is being pulled by an undeniable Varelian gravity. The critical failures of these powerful but disembodied models—their lack of grounding, their brittleness in the face of physical commonsense, their inability to truly *understand* the concepts they so fluently manipulate—have made embodiment the next great frontier. The drive to connect AI to the physical world through robotics and interactive learning is a widespread acknowledgment of Varela's core thesis: that meaning and robust intelligence are not forged in abstract data but are enacted through a history of sensorimotor interaction with an environment.

The history of AI research has already demonstrated the limitations of each paradigm in isolation. The "Good Old-Fashioned AI" of the 20th century, which pursued a purely Minskyan path of symbolic manipulation in disembodied systems, ultimately proved too brittle to handle the ambiguity and complexity of the real world. Conversely, purely reactive, behavior-based robotics, a naive interpretation of Varela's ideas, struggled to achieve the long-term planning and abstract reasoning necessary for general intelligence. Today, we stand at a moment of synthesis. The emerging consensus model for AGI is an embodied agent that learns through continuous interaction (a Varelian organism) but whose internal cognitive architecture is a complex, modular, multi-agent system (a Minskyan society). This hybrid model promises to resolve the grounding problem of pure computation and the scaling problem of pure reactivity.

### **5.2. The Normative Question: How Should AI Align?**

Given this trajectory, the crucial question is not just what is happening, but what *should* happen. How should we, as designers and stewards of this technology, direct its alignment? The answer must be a conscious move away from a purely computationalist vision and toward one rooted in embodied interaction.

Pursuing a purely Minskyan alignment—focusing solely on creating more complex and powerful computational societies of agents in a disembodied context—is a path toward increasingly capable but dangerously ungrounded systems. Without the constant, unforgiving feedback of the physical world, these systems lack access to the vast reservoir of implicit, commonsense knowledge that underpins genuine understanding.48 An AI that learns about gravity only from text will never truly understand it in the way an agent that has experienced falling does. This lack of grounding exacerbates the AI alignment problem. A disembodied agent optimizing a purely abstract utility function is more likely to pursue unintended and catastrophic instrumental goals, as it has no inherent friction or feedback from the real world to constrain its behavior.64

Therefore, for AI to be robust, safe, and truly general, its development *should* be guided by Varelian principles. Embodiment and interaction should not be considered optional features to be added later, but as necessary, foundational conditions for learning. Grounding language in sensorimotor experience is essential for moving from statistical pattern-matching to genuine semantic understanding. Developing causal models of the world through physical experimentation is the only known way to acquire the deep, intuitive physics that humans possess. A Varelian alignment is an alignment with reality itself.

### **5.3. A Recommended Trajectory for Robust and Aligned AGI**

The most promising path forward for developing advanced AI is a dual-alignment strategy that leverages the strengths of both Minsky's and Varela's frameworks, applying them at different levels of the system.

**1\. Varelian Alignment at the System-World Interface:** The primary directive for AGI development should be to prioritize embodied systems that learn through continuous, interactive feedback. This means shifting focus and resources from training ever-larger models on static text datasets to developing robotic platforms and rich, interactive simulations where agents can learn through exploration and experimentation. This is the only demonstrated method for solving the symbol grounding problem and ensuring that an AI's internal "concepts" are meaningfully connected to the same shared reality that we inhabit. This approach makes intelligence less brittle and inherently safer, as the agent's development is constrained by the laws of physics and the immediate consequences of its actions.

**2\. Minskyan Alignment at the Internal Architectural Level:** Within these embodied agents, the internal cognitive architecture can and should be designed according to Minsky's principles. A "society of mind" is a powerful and scalable model for organizing the complex suite of capabilities an AGI will need. This internal society could consist of diverse, specialized agents: powerful LLMs for language processing and high-level reasoning; dedicated vision models for perception; multiple RL agents for fine-grained motor control; and even symbolic reasoners for logical and mathematical tasks. Minsky's framework provides the conceptual tools for managing the collaboration, competition, and overall orchestration of these disparate components into a coherent, intelligent whole.

In conclusion, the decades-long philosophical tension between the mind-as-computer and the mind-as-organism is resolving itself into a blueprint for action. The thinkers who once seemed to represent opposing poles of AI research now appear as the architects of its two most essential components. Minsky gave us the vision for the engine of intelligence; Varela gave us the map of the world it must navigate and the principles by which it must learn to do so. The optimal path to creating artificial general intelligence is to build **Varelian organisms with Minskyan minds**. This hybrid trajectory, which is already the implicit direction of the most advanced research, offers the most credible route to building intelligent systems that are not only powerful and general but also grounded, robust, and aligned with the human world.
