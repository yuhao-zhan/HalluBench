**Unpacking Advanced Large Language Model Training Dynamics: Reinforcement Learning for Enhanced Capabilities and the Grokking Phenomenon**

**Abstract / Executive Summary**

This report examines two pivotal advancements in Large Language Model (LLM) training: the enhancement of complex problem-solving capabilities through Reinforcement Learning (RL) and the intriguing "Grokking" phenomenon. LLM capabilities, extending beyond mere text generation to intricate problem-solving, are increasingly critical for real-world applications. RL, particularly through methods like Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) and Constitutional AI, has shown promise in cultivating these advanced cognitive functions. Concurrently, the "Grokking" phenomenon, characterized by a delayed and abrupt generalization after extensive overfitting, challenges conventional understanding of model learning dynamics, particularly post-Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). This report explores the mechanisms, empirical evidence, and theoretical underpinnings of both, highlighting their implications for the development, evaluation, and fundamental understanding of advanced LLMs.

**1. Introduction**

**1.1 The Imperative of Advanced Capabilities in Large Language Models**

Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing, but their utility extends significantly when they demonstrate robust problem-solving abilities. This involves processing, interpreting, and establishing logical connections between various pieces of information to generate coherent and contextually relevant outputs, moving beyond surface-level language understanding.1 Such capabilities in LLMs emulate human-like problem-solving and decision-making, which are crucial for complex tasks such as mathematical computations, ethical dilemmas, and scientific inquiries.2 The ability to perform multi-step analysis and logical deduction is paramount for real-world applications across diverse domains.3

The assessment of LLM capabilities relies on standardized benchmarks that measure logical deduction, multi-step problem-solving, and structured thinking.2 These benchmarks, including HellaSwag, MMLU, SuperGLUE, BIG-Bench Hard, and MuSR, evaluate various facets of these abilities, from commonsense inference to complex logical puzzles.4 The operational definition of "LLM problem-solving" is thus implicitly shaped by what these benchmarks can effectively measure. This creates a critical feedback loop where benchmarks guide model improvements by revealing weaknesses, suggesting a co-evolution where the perceived progress can be influenced by the evaluation methodology itself. The continuous refinement of models to align with real-world applications and human expectations 2 indicates that the practical purpose of LLM capabilities—to enhance efficiency and decision-making in various industries—is a primary driver of research, prioritizing useful outcomes over abstract philosophical debates on the nature of intelligence.

**1.2 Overview of Reinforcement Learning and the Grokking Phenomenon**

Reinforcement Learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for fine-tuning LLMs, enabling them to learn from experience and optimize sequential decision-making to maximize long-term rewards.6 Its application to LLMs is driving significant improvements in generative AI, particularly for tasks requiring complex analysis.6

The "Grokking" phenomenon, or delayed generalization, is a counter-intuitive observation where a model abruptly transitions from overfitting (memorizing training data) to generalizing well on unseen data, long after its training loss has converged.8 This challenges traditional views on model training and generalization, suggesting that models may achieve a deeper understanding than previously thought, even after periods of apparent stagnation.

This report is structured to first elaborate on how RL enhances LLM capabilities, detailing various RL approaches and their empirical impact, alongside the critical challenges in evaluation. Subsequently, it provides a comprehensive analysis of the Grokking phenomenon, covering its definition, observed characteristics, and the evolving theoretical explanations. The report concludes by discussing the interplay between these two advanced training dynamics and outlining future research directions.

**2. Enhancing LLM Capabilities through Reinforcement Learning**

**2.1 Defining LLM Capabilities: Functions and Evaluation**

The ability of LLMs to process, interpret, and establish logical connections between disparate pieces of information to produce coherent and contextually relevant outputs is often referred to as LLM capabilities.1 This extends beyond simple text generation, leveraging advanced neural networks, particularly transformer models, to emulate problem-solving and decision-making processes.2

Key functions include:

* **Step-by-Step Problem Solving:** LLMs often employ an approach where a step-by-step logical progression is used to solve problems. This mirrors how humans break down complex problems into manageable parts and ensures clarity in analytical processes.2
* **Multi-Step Analysis:** LLMs can follow a sequence of analytical steps to address tasks requiring multiple layers of examination, such as mathematical computations, ethical dilemmas, or scientific inquiries.2
* **Contextual Understanding:** Through advanced language models like GPT-4 or PaLM, LLM engines contextualize information across a wide range of topics, enabling them to adapt their analytical approach based on the input provided.2

LLM capabilities are quantitatively assessed using various benchmarks that measure logical deduction, multi-step problem-solving, and structured thinking.2 Examples of these benchmarks include:

* **HellaSwag:** Designed to test commonsense natural language inference, requiring the model to predict the most likely ending of a sentence. It includes adversarial options that seem plausible, demanding deep analytical processing to rule out.5
* **Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU):** Evaluates LLMs' general knowledge and problem-solving across 57 subjects, including elementary mathematics, US history, computer science, and law. Scores are calculated as the percentage of correct answers.5
* **SuperGLUE:** An improved version of the GLUE benchmark, measuring how well LLMs handle a variety of real-world language tasks, such as understanding context, making inferences, and answering questions. A notable component is the Winograd Schema Challenge, which tests a model's ability to resolve ambiguities in pronoun references.4
* **BIG-Bench Hard & MuSR:** Specifically designed for advanced, multi-step analysis and complex logic puzzles, pushing LLMs beyond surface-level pattern recognition.4

The reliance on benchmarks for evaluating LLM capabilities highlights a critical feedback mechanism: benchmarks guide model improvements by identifying weaknesses. This suggests that the operational definition of "LLM capabilities" is implicitly shaped by what current benchmarks can effectively measure. If these benchmarks are limited or flawed, as some evidence suggests 11, then the perceived progress in these capabilities might, in part, be an artifact of the evaluation method rather than a fundamental gain. The continuous refinement of models to align with real-world applications and human expectations 2 indicates that the practical purpose of LLM capabilities—to enhance efficiency and decision-making in various industries—is a primary driver of research, prioritizing "useful" outcomes over abstract philosophical debates on true intelligence.

**2.2 Fundamentals of Reinforcement Learning in the LLM Context**

RL is an adaptive system where an "agent" (the LLM) learns to make sequential decisions by interacting with an "environment" to maximize cumulative "rewards".6

* **Agent:** The learning entity (LLM) that observes states, makes decisions, and takes actions (generates tokens or responses).6
* **Environment:** The external system (e.g., the task, the user query, a verification system) that responds to the agent's actions by transitioning to new states and providing rewards.6
* **State (Sₜ):** The current situation or configuration of the environment observed by the agent (e.g., the prompt, previous turns of conversation).6
* **Action (Aₜ):** The decision or move made by the agent at time t (e.g., generating the next token or a complete response).6
* **Reward (Rₜ):** Immediate feedback indicating the quality of an action, used to refine behavior over time.6

A crucial distinction exists between RL applied for complex problem-solving and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).12 While both incorporate RL, their objectives and reward mechanisms differ significantly. RL for problem-solving (sometimes referred to as "problem-solving LLMs") is specifically designed for tasks requiring logical thinking, such as mathematics or coding.12 RLHF, conversely, is for general-purpose alignment, making LLM responses helpful and preferred by users in typical conversational settings.6

In RL for problem-solving, the reward model is typically trained based on objective accuracy (e.g., code compilation, correct mathematical solution) or adherence to format (e.g., presence of specific internal thought tokens), often without direct human intervention in the reward model training.12 In contrast, RLHF explicitly involves human feedback (likes/dislikes, rankings) to train the reward model.6 The RLHF process generally involves three steps: collecting human feedback on model-generated responses, training a reward model to predict human preferences, and then fine-tuning the LLM using RL algorithms like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) or Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) to maximize this reward signal.6

This distinction highlights a fundamental difference in how "success" is defined: for problem-solving, it is about objective correctness; for general purpose, it is about subjective human preference. This suggests that the nature of the task dictates the most appropriate form of RL feedback. When objective ground truth is available (e.g., math problems), verifiable rewards are ideal. When subjective alignment is needed (e.g., harmlessness, helpfulness), human or AI-generated preferences are employed. The development of Constitutional AI 13 represents an attempt to bridge the gap between purely objective and purely subjective feedback by encoding "human values" into a "constitution" for AI-driven self-critique. This marks a significant step towards scalable alignment methods that reduce reliance on costly and potentially biased human labeling, while still aiming for complex behavioral shaping beyond simple correctness.

**2.3 Mechanisms of RL-Driven Capability Improvement**

**2.3.1 Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)**

RLVR applies reinforcement learning to an LLM using a rule-based outcome reward, typically a binary signal indicating the correctness of the model's final answer to a problem, such as a mathematical problem.15 This method effectively incentivizes mathematical problem-solving capabilities.15 Substantial improvements in mathematical problem-solving have been observed even with a single training example (1-shot RLVR) across various models (e.g., Qwen2.5-Math-7B, Llama3.2-3B-Instruct, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B) and RL algorithms (GRPO, PPO).15 For instance, 1-shot RLVR improved the Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B model from 36.0% to 73.6% on MATH500.15 The effectiveness of RLVR primarily stems from the policy gradient loss.15 Promoting exploration, often by incorporating entropy loss with an appropriate coefficient, plays a critical role in RLVR training.15 Interestingly, 1-shot RLVR has led to phenomena such as cross-domain generalization (improving non-mathematical problem-solving tasks) and sustained test performance improvement even after training accuracy saturation, a phenomenon termed "post-saturation generalization".15

**2.3.2 Multi-Attempt Reinforcement Learning and Self-Correction**

Traditional single-turn RL approaches for LLMs often suffer from sparse rewards, failing to train models to refine responses based on feedback.17 Multi-attempt RL addresses this limitation by allowing LLMs to make multiple attempts at solving a problem. In a multi-attempt task, the model receives feedback (e.g., +1 for correct, -0.5 for incorrect but well-formatted, and -1 otherwise) and can refine its responses iteratively.17 This iterative refinement, inspired by concepts like "Thinker" algorithms, enhances problem-solving and self-correction abilities.17 Empirical studies, such as DualityRL, have shown significant accuracy improvements, with a 45.6% to 52.5% gain with two attempts on math benchmarks, compared to marginal gains in single-turn models.17 This approach fosters emergent problem-solving capabilities through self-correction using algorithms like PPO.17 The progression from single-turn RL to multi-attempt RL demonstrates a critical evolution in how LLMs are trained for problem-solving. Single-turn approaches face challenges with sparse rewards, meaning the model receives very little feedback signal for complex, multi-step problems. Multi-attempt RL directly addresses this by creating a denser feedback loop, allowing the model to learn from intermediate steps and self-correct. This establishes a direct causal link between a training limitation (sparse rewards) and a methodological innovation (multi-attempt RL) leading to improved capabilities.

**2.3.3 Constitutional AI (RLAIF) for Aligned Capabilities**

Constitutional AI (CAI), introduced by Anthropic, offers an alternative to RLHF that aims for harmlessness and helpfulness without direct human feedback.13 It utilizes a "constitution" of human-written principles (e.g., "most helpful, honest, and harmless") against which the LLM evaluates its own outputs.13 The process involves two stages:

* **Phase 1 (Supervised Learning):** The model learns to critique and revise its own harmful responses iteratively based on constitutional principles.13
* **Phase 2 (Reinforcement Learning):** The AI model generates preference data by evaluating responses against the constitutional principles. This data is then used to train a new model via Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF), reinforcing constitution-aligned behavior.13

CAI claims superiority over RLHF in terms of efficiency, transparency, and objectivity.13 It allows for shaping not just

*what* content is refused, but *how* that refusal is communicated, maintaining user engagement.14 CAI has demonstrated overall harm reduction in revised responses for small uncensored models (DeepSeek-R1, Gemma-2, Llama 3.1).14 However, its success can depend on the model's inherent capacity for accurate self-assessment, as evidenced by Qwen2.5's limited improvement due to its failure in identifying harmful content.14 Smaller models generally require more explicit guidance and structured prompts to achieve comparable results.14 The focus on "self-correction" and "self-critique" mechanisms across different RL approaches for problem-solving points towards a future where LLMs are not just trained to

*produce* correct answers, but to *evaluate and refine* their own internal processes. This could lead to more robust and reliable agents, potentially reducing the need for extensive external validation loops in certain applications.

**2.4 Empirical Evidence and Challenges in RL-Enhanced Capabilities**

Observed Performance Gains:

* RLVR has shown substantial improvements in mathematical problem-solving, with 1-shot training on specific examples leading to significant percentage point increases on benchmarks like MATH500.15
* Cross-domain generalization has been observed, where RLVR on math examples can improve performance on non-mathematical problem-solving tasks.15
* Multi-attempt RL has demonstrated accuracy improvements of 45.6% to 52.5% on math benchmarks with just two attempts, indicating effective iterative refinement.17
* Constitutional AI has shown overall harm reduction in model responses through self-critique.14
* RL has been leveraged to create sophisticated LLM-based search agents that combine problem-solving with search engine use, with format rewards proving effective.7

Factors Influencing RL Outcomes:

* **Reward Formulation:** Format rewards are effective, while intermediate retrieval rewards have limited impact.7
* **LLM Backbone:** The scale and initialization of the LLM (general-purpose vs. specialized for problem-solving) significantly influence RL outcomes, with general-purpose LLMs sometimes outperforming specialized ones in RL settings.7 Scaling up generally enhances performance, though with diminishing returns.7
* **Search Engine Choice:** The quality of the search engine used during training critically shapes RL dynamics and agent robustness.7

Challenges and Limitations:

A significant challenge is the statistical fragility of LLM capability benchmarks.11 Many reported RL gains can fall within random variance, rendering empirical conclusions unreliable.11 This observation highlights a critical need for methodological rigor over leaderboard competition in LLM research. It suggests that the current rapid pace of LLM development, driven by quick publication cycles and benchmarking competitions, may be generating misleading results. For practitioners, this implies that reported benchmark scores, especially for RL-enhanced models, should be viewed with skepticism and validated under diverse, controlled conditions.

LLM pipelines are highly sensitive to minor design choices, including decoding parameters, prompt formatting, random seeds, and hardware configurations.11 This leads to significant performance variance (5-15 percentage points standard deviation) across random seeds.11 This sensitivity suggests that the learned capabilities might be highly brittle or context-dependent, rather than truly robust and generalizable. A model might perform well under one specific set of evaluation conditions but fail under slightly different ones, making real-world deployment challenging.

RL-trained models can exhibit problematic overfitting to training distributions, showing pronounced performance drops between easier and more challenging benchmarks (e.g., AIME'24 vs. AIME'25).11 While RL can substantially improve base model performance (e.g., Qwen2.5), instruction tuning often remains superior for robust, generalizable performance gains.11 Furthermore, problem-solving LLMs can take longer to generate responses, even for simple queries, increasing inference time.12

**3. The Grokking Phenomenon in LLMs after Supervised Fine-Tuning**

**3.1 Defining Grokking: Delayed Generalization**

Grokking, also known as delayed generalization, is a counter-intuitive phenomenon in machine learning where a model abruptly transitions from overfitting (performing well only on training data) to generalizing (performing well on both training and test data).8 This transition occurs after many training iterations during which there appears to be little progress in generalization.8 The phenomenon was formally introduced in January 2022 by OpenAI researchers who observed it while investigating how neural networks perform calculations.8 The term "grokking" is derived from Robert Heinlein's novel, signifying a deep and thorough understanding.8

A core characteristic of grokking is its challenge to the conventional wisdom of early stopping, as significant generalization can occur long after training loss has converged or plateaued—a widely accepted indicator for stopping training.10 It is often described as an "aha moment" where the model suddenly grasps the underlying principles rather than just memorizing examples.9 This directly contradicts the fundamental assumption in traditional machine learning that overfitting is a "dead end" 9 and that generalization performance typically deteriorates after training loss converges. The observation that grokking occurs "without special techniques" and often with "standard optimization methods, often with weight decay as the only regularization" 9 suggests that it is an intrinsic property of deep learning dynamics, implying that our understanding of how deep networks learn and generalize is still incomplete. Grokking forces a re-evaluation of how model generalization is understood, suggesting that models might achieve a deeper, more robust understanding than previously thought, even after periods of apparent stagnation or memorization. This has profound implications for designing training schedules, monitoring model progress, and interpreting learning curves, especially for large-scale foundation models where training is computationally expensive and long-running.

**3.2 Characteristics and Empirical Observations**

Grokking is typically characterized by a delayed generalization curve: training accuracy becomes near perfect relatively early (e.g., < 10^3 optimization steps), but validation accuracy (generalization) only reaches that level much later (e.g., ~10^6 steps), with very little evidence of generalization until a significantly delayed point.21 This reveals a significant temporal decoupling between memorization and generalization. The model quickly learns to perfectly fit the training data (memorization), but the deeper, generalizable understanding takes orders of magnitude more training. This implies that fast memorization might be a prerequisite or a side-effect of the initial learning phase, but it does not equate to true understanding.

A notable relationship exists with dataset size: the optimization time required to reach generalization (e.g., 99% validation accuracy) increases rapidly, often exponentially, as the training data fraction decreases.21 Conversely, the time to achieve high training accuracy generally trends down with smaller dataset sizes.21 This suggests that smaller datasets, while allowing for quicker memorization, make the discovery of underlying generalizable patterns significantly harder or more time-consuming. This establishes a causal relationship between data scarcity and the difficulty of achieving deep generalization, even if the final performance is perfect. It implies that for certain tasks, a minimum "signal density" in the training data might be required for efficient generalization. This characteristic of grokking suggests that simply achieving low training loss is an insufficient metric for assessing a model's true learning progress. Researchers and practitioners must monitor generalization performance over much longer training horizons, especially for tasks where underlying rules or structures need to be deeply understood. This has direct implications for resource allocation in training large models.

Grokking has been observed in deep neural networks and non-neural models 8, including one-layer Transformers 20 and on algorithmic datasets like modular addition/division 8, as well as on datasets like MNIST.20 It can occur across a range of models, optimizers, and dataset sizes.21

While both involve unexpected performance improvements, Grokking is distinct from the Double Descent phenomenon.19 Grokking involves a

*sudden* improvement after a prolonged period of suboptimal performance within a fixed model structure, focusing on the learning process.19 Double Descent describes a non-monotonic relationship between

*model complexity* and performance, where performance degrades at the interpolation threshold but then unexpectedly improves as complexity *continues to increase*.19 Grokking happens after

*extensive training time*, whereas Double Descent occurs as model complexity is *varied*.19

**3.3 Theoretical Explanations and Underlying Mechanisms**

**3.3.1 Phase Transitions and Training Regimes**

Grokking can be understood as a phase transition during the training process, specifically a "complexity phase transition" in the model.8 Recent theories hypothesize that grokking occurs when neural networks transition from a "lazy training" regime to a "rich" regime.8 In the "lazy regime," the network behaves like a linearized model, where weights do not deviate far from initialization, and learning occurs without significantly changing internal features.8 The network might imperfectly memorize the training set in this regime.20 In contrast, in the "rich regime," weights abruptly begin to move in task-relevant directions, adapting features to the data, leading to improved generalization at a later stage.8 This transition is influenced by adaptive optimizers, weight decay, and initial parameter weight norm.8

**3.3.2 Role of Weight Decay and Delayed Feature Learning**

Weight decay (a regularization component penalizing higher parameter values) is often present during grokking.8 One potential explanation is that it slightly favors the general solution, which involves lower weight values but is harder to find.8 The weight decay term encourages uniformity across all tokens in the embedding space when minimized, and this embedding uniformity, combined with the training dataset distribution, jointly determines the occurrence of grokking.22 A prominent theory suggests grokking is caused by delayed feature learning during training.20 The network first attempts to fit a kernel regression solution with its initial features, followed by late-time feature learning where a generalizing solution is discovered.20

**3.3.3 Emergence of Robust Input Space Partition and Pathway Evolution**

Grokking occurs due to the emergence of a robust input space partition by a Deep Neural Network (DNN), through a linearization of the DNN function around training points as a consequence of training dynamics.23 Prior to grokking, the network undergoes a phase change in local complexity.23 A novel form of grokking, termed "delayed robustness," has been observed, where DNNs generalize to adversarial samples long after generalizing on the test dataset.23

For practical, large-scale foundation models, grokking has been witnessed during pretraining, though different data may enter their grokking stages asynchronously, a phenomenon referred to as "local grokking".10 The "emergence of generalization" during grokking can be understood by investigating the dynamics of LLMs' internal states, such as Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) pathways.10 Training samples' pathways evolve from random, instance-specific to more structured and shareable between samples.10 The complexity of a single sample's pathway reduces despite the converged loss.10 This evolution reflects a "smarter memorization" that discovers and leverages more shareable knowledge across samples, leading to more efficient encoding of each individual sample. This explains why generalization improves without reducing the training loss: the model finds more generalizable and shareable representations.10 Pathway complexity and consistency show strong correlations with test performance after instruction fine-tuning, providing efficient, test-set/finetuning-free tools to monitor generalization during pretraining.10

The various theoretical explanations (phase transitions, lazy-to-rich regimes, delayed feature learning, embedding uniformity, robust input space partition, pathway evolution) are not mutually exclusive but rather describe different facets of a deeper underlying process: the model's gradual discovery and internal restructuring of abstract, generalizable representations. The "aha moment" 9 is not a sudden random event but the culmination of a long, subtle process of weight adjustment and feature refinement, driven by factors like weight decay.8 The finding that grokking occurs in large-scale foundation models and involves the evolution of "pathways...from random, instance-specific to more structured and shareable" 10 is particularly significant. This suggests that grokking is not just a phenomenon of small, algorithmic datasets but a fundamental aspect of how complex models learn to generalize on real-world data. The reduction in "pathway complexity" and increase in "consistency" 10 implies that the model is learning a more compressed, efficient, and robust internal representation of the data, which is the essence of generalization. The ability to monitor generalization during pretraining using metrics like pathway complexity and consistency 10 could dramatically optimize the training of massive LLMs, saving significant computational resources and time, by moving beyond simply observing grokking to actively leveraging its underlying mechanisms for more efficient and effective model development.

**3.4 Implications for LLM Training, Evaluation, and Generalization**

Grokking challenges the widely-used practice of early stopping based on validation loss or training loss convergence.10 Optimal training might require significantly longer durations to unlock full generalization capabilities, especially for tasks requiring deep understanding. This suggests that simply training to convergence might leave significant untapped generalization potential. This means that current LLMs might be "under-grokking" their data, and longer, more resource-intensive training runs could yield disproportionately better models, directly impacting the economic and environmental costs of LLM development.

Traditional metrics like training and validation loss are insufficient to capture grokking.8 Novel internal state metrics, such as pathway complexity and consistency 10, offer a more efficient and insightful way to monitor generalization during pretraining, potentially eliminating the need for constant test-set evaluations.

Grokking provides valuable insights into the mechanisms of model generalization and representation learning.10 It suggests that generalization is not merely about fitting data but about discovering underlying, often simpler, rules or structures.9 The observation that grokking diminishes as the number of transformer layers increases 22 suggests that deeper architectures might possess greater knowledge capacity, but also that the specific mechanisms of generalization might change with scale. The "delayed robustness" phenomenon 23 indicates that grokking might also be linked to the emergence of robustness to adversarial samples, adding another dimension to the benefits of extended training. Grokking, alongside other phenomena like neural collapse and scaling laws 24, contributes to a broader understanding of "emergent behaviors" in deep neural networks. It suggests that complex, intelligent behaviors might not be explicitly programmed but rather "emerge" from the training dynamics themselves, given sufficient data, model capacity, and training time. This fuels the debate on the nature of intelligence in AI systems.

**4. Interplay and Future Directions**

**4.1 Synergies and Distinctions between RL-Enhanced Capabilities and Grokking**

RL-enhanced capabilities represent a *deliberate training methodology* applied to improve specific functions, often guided by explicit reward signals.15 Grokking, conversely, is an

*emergent phenomenon* observed during training (often SFT) that reveals how models generalize, irrespective of the specific fine-tuning objective.8

Despite these distinctions, potential synergies exist. Understanding grokking's mechanisms, particularly the "lazy-to-rich" transition and pathway evolution 8, could inform more efficient RL training schedules for capability enhancement. If RL-trained models also exhibit grokking, identifying its onset could signal optimal points for stopping or modifying training. The "delayed robustness" aspect of grokking 23 suggests that extended training might not only improve capabilities but also make them more robust to adversarial inputs, a desirable outcome for RL-trained agents. Furthermore, the pathway complexity and consistency metrics identified for monitoring grokking 10 could potentially be applied to RL-enhanced models to track their generalization progress more effectively, especially given the statistical fragility of current benchmarks.11

While RL is a *methodology* and Grokking is an *emergent property*, they are not entirely separate. If RL-trained models (which are often fine-tuned from SFT models) also exhibit grokking, then the mechanisms of grokking could provide a deeper understanding of *how* RL leads to generalization in complex tasks, beyond simply maximizing reward. For example, does RL expedite the "lazy-to-rich" transition or encourage the formation of "structured and shareable pathways" for problem-solving? The statistical fragility of RL benchmarks 11 and the overfitting observed in RL-trained models 11 suggest that current RL methods might be achieving superficial gains or memorizing specific test distributions. If grokking is about achieving

*true* generalization 9, then observing grokking in RL-trained models for complex tasks would be a stronger indicator of robust learning than benchmark scores alone. This implies a causal link where understanding grokking can help diagnose and improve the generalization quality of RL-enhanced capabilities. The interplay suggests a future where LLM training is not just about applying a technique (like RL) but about understanding and manipulating the

*underlying learning dynamics* (like grokking) to achieve truly robust and generalizable capabilities. This calls for a more unified theoretical framework that integrates observations from both areas.

**4.2 Open Questions and Future Research Avenues**

Several open questions and promising research avenues emerge from this analysis:

* **Predicting Grokking:** Can the onset of grokking be reliably predicted in large, complex LLMs, especially given the "local" and "asynchronous" nature observed in foundation models?10
* **Optimizing Training for Grokking:** How can training procedures be explicitly designed or adapted to accelerate or guarantee grokking for specific capabilities, rather than waiting for it to emerge spontaneously?
* **RL and Grokking Interaction:** Does RL (e.g., RLVR, multi-attempt RL) influence the occurrence or characteristics of grokking in LLMs? Can RL be used to *induce* grokking-like generalization for specific tasks?
* **Robustness of RL Gains:** How can the statistical fragility of LLM capability benchmarks 11 be overcome to ensure that reported RL gains reflect genuine, robust improvements rather than artifacts of evaluation? This necessitates more rigorous methodologies, diverse datasets, and multi-seed evaluations.
* **Scaling Grokking Observations:** How do the theoretical explanations of grokking, largely derived from smaller models and algorithmic datasets, scale to the complexity of large-scale foundation models and real-world tasks?
* **Unified Theory of Generalization:** Can a comprehensive theoretical framework be developed that unifies phenomena like grokking, double descent 19, neural collapse, and emergent abilities 24 to provide a more holistic understanding of deep learning generalization?

**5. Conclusion**

The advancement of problem-solving abilities in Large Language Models is a critical frontier, with Reinforcement Learning emerging as a powerful, albeit complex, tool. Techniques like Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR), multi-attempt RL, and Constitutional AI (RLAIF) demonstrate varied success in fostering logical deduction, multi-step problem-solving, and self-correction. However, the empirical evidence is nuanced, with significant challenges regarding benchmark fragility and potential overfitting, underscoring the need for more rigorous evaluation methodologies.

Concurrently, the "Grokking" phenomenon presents a profound challenge and opportunity, revealing that deep generalization can occur long after apparent overfitting. Its theoretical explanations, ranging from phase transitions in training dynamics (lazy-to-rich regimes) to the evolution of internal representations and pathways, offer invaluable understandings into how LLMs move from rote memorization to genuine understanding. The observation of "local grokking" in large foundation models and the potential for internal state monitoring (e.g., pathway complexity) hold significant promise for optimizing future LLM training.

The interplay between RL-enhanced capabilities and the grokking phenomenon suggests a deeper, more intricate relationship between training methodologies and emergent generalization. Future research must bridge these areas, leveraging observations from grokking to refine RL strategies for more robust and truly generalizable capabilities in LLMs, ultimately advancing the frontier of artificial intelligence.
