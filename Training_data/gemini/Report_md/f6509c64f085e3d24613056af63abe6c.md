

# **Pyrolysis of Pecan Shells for Advanced Carbon Materials: A Comprehensive Analysis of Temperature Effects, Physicochemical Properties, and Characterization for Electrical and Sensing Applications**

## **Section 1: Pecan Shell Biochar as a Functional Carbon Material**

### **1.1. Introduction: Valorization of Lignocellulosic Waste**

The global imperative for a circular economy and sustainable resource management has catalyzed intensive research into the valorization of agricultural and industrial byproducts. Among these, pecan shells (*Carya illinoinensis*) represent a significant and underutilized lignocellulosic waste stream.1 Global pecan production generates millions of tons of shells annually, which are typically disposed of in landfills, burned inefficiently in orchards, or used in low-value applications.1 Such disposal methods not only represent a loss of a valuable carbonaceous resource but can also lead to environmental issues, including water pollution and greenhouse gas emissions.1 The conversion of this abundant biomass into high-value functional materials is therefore a critical objective for both waste management and advanced materials engineering.

Thermochemical conversion processes, particularly pyrolysis, offer a robust and scalable pathway to transform low-value lignocellulosic biomass into a suite of valuable products, including bio-oil, syngas, and a solid, carbon-rich material known as biochar.3 Pecan nutshells (PNS) are an exceptionally suitable precursor for this process. The elemental composition of PNS reveals a high carbon content, typically around 53.1%, with the remainder being primarily oxygen (44.7%) and minor inorganic components.1 Structurally, PNS is composed of the three primary biopolymers that constitute plant cell walls: cellulose (approximately 32.9%), hemicellulose (approximately 22.5%), and a significant fraction of lignin.1 This specific composition, particularly its high lignin content, makes PNS an ideal feedstock for producing structurally stable and highly aromatic carbon materials, which are precursors to advanced functional carbons.6 The high density and low inherent ash content of pecan shells further enhance their attractiveness, as these properties translate to higher yields of granular carbon materials with fewer impurities compared to other agricultural residues like cotton gin trash or animal manures.6

### **1.2. From Biomass to Biochar: The Pyrolytic Transformation**

Pyrolysis is fundamentally the thermal decomposition of organic material in an oxygen-deficient or completely anoxic atmosphere, a process that prevents combustion and instead promotes the cleavage of chemical bonds to yield solid, liquid, and gaseous products.3 The transformation of pecan shell biomass into biochar is a complex, multi-stage process governed by the distinct thermal stabilities of its primary components: hemicellulose, cellulose, and lignin. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of PNS reveals a sequential degradation profile.1 The process begins with the evaporation of residual moisture at temperatures up to approximately 150°C. The first major stage of devolatilization occurs between 250°C and 315°C, corresponding to the decomposition of the amorphous, branched hemicellulose polymers.1 This is followed by the rapid and more complete degradation of the crystalline cellulose component, which occurs in a narrower and higher temperature range, typically from 348°C to 385°C.1 Lignin, an amorphous and highly cross-linked polymer composed of phenylpropane units, is the most thermally recalcitrant component. Its decomposition occurs over a very broad temperature range, beginning around 280°C and continuing up to 900°C or higher, contributing significantly to the final biochar yield and its aromatic structure.5

The final properties of the biochar are not merely a function of the feedstock but are critically dictated by the pyrolysis process parameters, with temperature being the most influential variable.2 Pyrolysis temperature governs the extent of devolatilization, the degree of secondary reactions (such as tar cracking and repolymerization), and the ultimate rearrangement of the carbon matrix. Lower temperatures (e.g., \< 500°C) result in incomplete carbonization, yielding a biochar with a higher mass yield but retaining a significant portion of the original biomass's aliphatic character and oxygen-containing functional groups. Conversely, higher temperatures (e.g., \> 700°C) drive off nearly all non-carbon elements, leading to a highly carbonaceous, aromatic, and increasingly ordered structure, albeit at the expense of a lower solid yield.6 Other parameters, such as heating rate and residence time, also play significant roles; slower heating rates generally favor higher char yields, while longer residence times at the target temperature ensure more complete conversion.3 By precisely controlling these parameters, it is possible to tailor the physicochemical properties of pecan shell biochar for specific, high-performance applications.

### **1.3. Pecan Shell Biochar for Advanced Applications: Rationale and Potential**

The true value of pecan shell biochar lies in its potential to be engineered into a functional carbon material for advanced technological applications, particularly in the fields of energy storage and electrochemical sensing. These applications have stringent material requirements that commodity carbons cannot meet. For instance, high-performance supercapacitor electrodes, which store energy via the formation of an electric double-layer at the electrode-electrolyte interface, demand materials with an exceptionally high specific surface area (SSA), a hierarchical pore structure that facilitates rapid ion transport, and high electrical conductivity to minimize internal resistance and enable high power density.14 Similarly, the performance of electrochemical sensors, which detect analytes by measuring changes in electrical signals, relies on electrode materials that offer a high SSA to maximize interaction sites, good electrical conductivity for efficient signal transduction, and specific surface functional groups that can enhance selectivity or serve as anchors for immobilizing recognition elements like enzymes or antibodies.16

Pecan shell biochar is a compelling candidate to meet these demands. Its inherent low ash content ensures that the resulting material is primarily carbonaceous, avoiding the insulating or interfering effects of high mineral content.8 The significant lignin fraction of the parent biomass provides a robust structural backbone that resists pore collapse during high-temperature pyrolysis.6 This structural stability is a fundamental prerequisite for developing the high surface area and interconnected porosity essential for supercapacitor performance. The ability to tune the properties of pecan shell biochar over a wide range—from a functional-group-rich material at low temperatures to a highly conductive, graphitic-like material at high temperatures—makes it a versatile platform for creating customized carbon materials. Through pyrolysis, with or without subsequent activation steps, this abundant agricultural waste can be transformed into a cost-effective, sustainable alternative to conventional carbon materials like activated carbon, carbon black, or even more advanced materials like graphene, for a new generation of electronic devices.4

## **Section 2: The Critical Influence of Pyrolysis Temperature on Biochar Properties**

The pyrolysis temperature is the single most dominant process variable in determining the final physicochemical characteristics of biochar. Its influence extends from the macroscopic property of mass yield to the nanoscale properties of pore structure, surface chemistry, and the atomic arrangement of the carbon matrix. The evolution of these properties with temperature is not always linear but often occurs in distinct stages that are directly linked to the thermal decomposition of the primary lignocellulosic components of the pecan shell feedstock. Understanding these temperature-dependent transformations is crucial for engineering biochar with properties tailored for specific applications, such as the high conductivity required for electrodes or the specific surface chemistry needed for sensors. This section provides a systematic analysis of how key biochar properties evolve as a function of pyrolysis temperature.

### **2.1. Biochar Yield and Carbonization Efficiency**

A fundamental and universally observed relationship in pyrolysis is the inverse correlation between the final pyrolysis temperature and the solid biochar yield. As the temperature increases, a greater fraction of the initial biomass is converted into volatile components (bio-oil and syngas), resulting in a lower mass of the solid carbonaceous residue. Studies on pecan shells and other nut shells consistently demonstrate this trend. At lower pyrolysis temperatures, in the range of 300-500°C, biochar yields are relatively high, often exceeding 50% of the initial dry biomass weight.6 This is because at these temperatures, the primary mass loss is driven by the decomposition of the less stable hemicellulose and cellulose components, while the more recalcitrant lignin fraction remains largely intact as a solid char.1

As the pyrolysis temperature is elevated into the mid-range (500-750°C), the yield progressively decreases, typically falling into the 30-50% range.6 This continued mass loss is attributable to the ongoing, slower decomposition of lignin and secondary reactions where previously formed char and tars are further cracked into lower molecular weight gases.5 At high temperatures, above 750°C, the biochar yield stabilizes at a lower value, often below 30%.6 For example, one study reported that the mass yield of biochars prepared from various feedstocks, including pecan shells, was at least 50% below 500°C but decreased to about 30% as the temperature was raised to 750°C.6 This phenomenon reflects the near-complete devolatilization of the organic matter, leaving behind a highly carbon-rich and structurally condensed material. While a higher temperature reduces the overall mass yield, it simultaneously increases the carbonization efficiency, meaning the resulting biochar has a much higher percentage of fixed carbon and a more stable, aromatic structure. This trade-off between yield and carbon quality is a central consideration in biochar production.

### **2.2. Evolution of Porous Architecture: Specific Surface Area (BET) and Porosity**

The development of a porous structure is one of the most significant transformations that occurs during pyrolysis, and it is profoundly influenced by temperature. The specific surface area (SSA), typically measured by the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method using nitrogen gas adsorption, is a critical parameter for applications requiring a large interfacial area, such as supercapacitors and sensors. For pecan shell biochar, there is a clear and positive correlation between pyrolysis temperature and SSA, up to an optimal point.

At low pyrolysis temperatures (\< 500°C), the release of volatiles is incomplete, and the resulting biochar often has a poorly developed pore structure and consequently a low SSA. Reported values in this range can be as low as 8.57–22.31 m²/g.3 As the temperature increases into the 500-800°C range, the progressive and more vigorous release of volatile matter from the lignocellulosic matrix acts as a pore-forming mechanism, creating and expanding a network of micropores and mesopores. This leads to a dramatic increase in both SSA and total pore volume.2 For instance, studies have reported SSA values for pecan shell biochar of 93 m²/g (pyrolyzed at 800°C) 21, 185 m²/g at 750°C 6, and as high as 450.23 m²/g for a sample prepared at 800°C.2 This enhancement is attributed to the complete removal of pore-blocking tars and the structural rearrangement of the carbon matrix.

However, this trend does not continue indefinitely. At very high temperatures (e.g., above 900°C), a phenomenon of structural annealing and shrinkage can occur. The carbon matrix can begin to densify and graphitize, leading to the collapse of micropores and a subsequent decrease in SSA and pore volume.2 This suggests the existence of an optimal temperature for maximizing porosity. For pecan shell-derived activated carbon, where pyrolysis is coupled with an activation step (e.g., chemical or physical activation), exceptionally high SSAs can be achieved. One study on a self-activated pecan shell carbon produced at 1050°C reported a remarkable BET surface area of 1554 m²/g, highlighting the material's immense potential for high-performance applications.14 The development of a hierarchical pore structure, containing both micropores (\< 2 nm) for high charge density and mesopores (2-50 nm) for efficient ion transport, is ideal for supercapacitors and is also a direct function of the pyrolysis and activation conditions.14

### **2.3. Transformation of Surface Chemistry (FTIR & XPS Analysis)**

Pyrolysis temperature fundamentally alters the surface chemistry of biochar, transitioning it from a polar, functionalized material to a nonpolar, aromatic one. This transformation can be tracked qualitatively by Fourier-Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR) and quantitatively by X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS).

FTIR analysis of biochar produced at lower temperatures (\< 500°C) reveals a surface rich in oxygen-containing functional groups inherited from the original cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin. Spectra in this range typically show a broad absorption band around 3400 cm⁻¹ corresponding to hydroxyl (-OH) stretching in alcohols and carboxylic acids, as well as peaks for carbonyl (C=O) stretching near 1700 cm⁻¹ and C-O stretching in ethers and esters around 1100 cm⁻¹.3 As the pyrolysis temperature is increased, these functional groups become thermally unstable and are progressively removed through dehydration and decarboxylation reactions. Consequently, in biochars produced at high temperatures (\> 750°C), the intensity of these oxygen-related peaks is significantly reduced or they disappear entirely. Concurrently, the absorption peak near 1600 cm⁻¹, associated with aromatic C=C ring stretching, becomes more prominent, signifying the formation of a highly aromatic, poly-condensed carbon structure.3

XPS provides more detailed, quantitative insight into this chemical evolution. By analyzing the high-resolution spectra of core-level electrons, XPS can determine both the surface elemental composition and the chemical bonding states of those elements. For pecan shell biochar, as pyrolysis temperature increases, the atomic percentage of oxygen on the surface decreases sharply, while the percentage of carbon increases, leading to a much higher C/O ratio.2 The deconvolution of the high-resolution C 1s spectrum is particularly revealing. In low-temperature biochar, the C 1s peak shows significant contributions from carbon bonded to oxygen in various forms, such as C-O (hydroxyl, ether) and C=O (carbonyl, carboxyl). In high-temperature biochar, the C 1s spectrum becomes dominated by the peak corresponding to sp²-hybridized graphitic carbon (C-C/C=C), with only minor contributions from oxidized carbon species.24 This quantitative shift confirms the transformation into a carbon-pure, aromatic material, which is a prerequisite for achieving high electrical conductivity.

### **2.4. Structural Ordering and Degree of Graphitization (Raman Analysis)**

While XPS and FTIR describe the surface chemistry, Raman spectroscopy probes the underlying structure and order of the carbon matrix itself. It is an indispensable tool for characterizing the degree of graphitization in carbon materials. The Raman spectrum of biochar is typically dominated by two main features: the G-band (Graphitic) appearing around 1580 cm⁻¹, which arises from the in-plane bond-stretching motion of pairs of sp²-hybridized carbon atoms in aromatic rings and is a signature of ordered graphitic lattices; and the D-band (Disorder) around 1350 cm⁻¹, which is a breathing mode of sp² rings that becomes active only in the presence of defects, such as edges, vacancies, or amorphous carbon structures.26

The intensity ratio of these two bands, the Iᴅ/Iɢ ratio, is a widely used metric to assess the degree of structural order. For biochar, the interpretation of this ratio is nuanced. In the transition from amorphous biomass to nanocrystalline carbon, an increase in the Iᴅ/Iɢ ratio with pyrolysis temperature indicates the formation and growth of small graphitic domains within the larger amorphous carbon matrix. As temperature rises, the amorphous carbon network derived from the lignocellulosic precursors begins to rearrange into more stable, poly-condensed aromatic ring systems or "graphene-like" clusters. The D-band intensity increases because of the proliferation of edges and boundaries of these newly formed nanocrystallites. Therefore, a higher Iᴅ/Iɢ ratio in biochar produced at higher temperatures signifies not more disorder in the traditional sense, but a greater prevalence of small, ordered graphitic clusters, which is a key step in the graphitization process.27 This structural evolution from a highly disordered, insulating material to one containing a network of conductive graphitic domains is the fundamental reason for the emergence of electrical conductivity at high pyrolysis temperatures.

### **2.5. Emergence of Electrical Conductivity**

The electrical conductivity of biochar is a direct consequence of its atomic structure and chemical composition, both of which are controlled by the pyrolysis temperature. Raw biomass and low-temperature biochar are electrical insulators. Their carbon atoms are primarily sp³-hybridized or are part of sp² systems that are isolated by oxygen-containing functional groups, preventing the formation of delocalized π-electron systems necessary for charge transport.

As the pyrolysis temperature increases, a profound transformation occurs. The process of carbonization and aromatization, confirmed by FTIR, XPS, and Raman spectroscopy, creates an extended, interconnected network of sp²-hybridized carbon atoms. In these graphitic domains, the π-orbitals of adjacent carbon atoms overlap, creating delocalized electron clouds that can move freely across the structure when an electric field is applied.7 The progressive removal of insulating oxygen-containing functional groups is equally critical, as these groups act as scattering centers that impede electron flow.7 Therefore, the increase in electrical conductivity with temperature is a synergistic effect of two concurrent processes: the formation of conductive sp² carbon pathways and the removal of insulating chemical species.

Experimental data consistently show that the electrical conductivity (EC) of biochar increases by several orders of magnitude as the pyrolysis temperature is raised from 400°C to over 800°C.6 For instance, one study on walnut shell char found that samples prepared at or above 800°C behaved as semiconductors with conductivities ranging from 44 to 510 S/m.29 An interesting comparative observation is that pecan shell biochar tends to have a lower EC than biochars derived from feedstocks with high ash content, such as cotton gin trash.11 This suggests that the conductivity of pecan shell biochar is intrinsically derived from its carbon structure, rather than being augmented by conductive mineral phases present in the ash. This makes it a "purer" and potentially more predictable carbon conductor for electronic applications where the properties of the carbon matrix itself are paramount.

Instead, a suite of complementary characterization techniques is required to build a holistic picture of the material, from its macroscopic morphology down to its atomic structure and surface chemistry. Each technique provides a unique piece of the puzzle, and their combined results allow for the establishment of clear structure-property relationships. This section provides a comparative analysis of six critical techniques—SEM, Raman spectroscopy, FTIR/XPS, BET, TGA, and four-probe conductivity measurement—explaining the specific information each provides and its direct relevance to evaluating biochar for advanced applications. ### **3.1. Morphological and Topographical Analysis (SEM)**

Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is an imaging technique that utilizes a focused beam of high-energy electrons to scan the surface of a sample. The interactions between the electrons and the atoms of the sample produce various signals—primarily secondary electrons and backscattered electrons—that can be collected to form a high-resolution, three-dimensional image of the surface topography.2

For biochar characterization, SEM is invaluable for providing direct visual evidence of the structural transformations that occur during pyrolysis. SEM micrographs can clearly resolve the macro- and mesoporous structures, revealing the channels, cavities, and pores that form as volatile matter is released from the biomass matrix.2 It allows for the qualitative assessment of how pyrolysis temperature affects the porous architecture; images of low-temperature biochar often show a dense, non-porous surface resembling the original feedstock, while high-temperature biochar exhibits a much looser, more open, and honeycomb-like structure.2 SEM can also reveal particle size, shape, and the degree of surface roughness, all of which are important for electrode fabrication and performance. In the context of electrical and sensing applications, SEM is crucial for verifying the creation of a porous network. For supercapacitors, this porous structure is essential for maximizing the accessible surface area for the electrolyte ions. For sensors, it provides the physical channels for analytes to reach the active sensing sites on the biochar surface.

### **3.2. Probing Carbon Structure and Order (Raman Spectroscopy)**

Raman spectroscopy is a non-destructive spectroscopic technique based on the inelastic scattering of monochromatic light, usually from a laser source. When photons interact with a material, most are scattered elastically (Rayleigh scattering) with no change in energy. A small fraction, however, is scattered inelastically (Raman scattering), with the energy difference between the incident and scattered photons corresponding to the energy of a specific vibrational mode of the molecules in the sample.34 This technique is particularly powerful for carbon materials because it is highly sensitive to the symmetry and bonding arrangement of carbon atoms (e.g., sp² vs. sp³ hybridization) and the degree of long-range structural order.26

As discussed in Section 2.4, Raman spectroscopy provides the characteristic D-band and G-band, which are fundamental to understanding the carbon structure of biochar. The G-band at \~1580 cm⁻¹ corresponds to the ideal graphitic lattice vibration, while the D-band at \~1350 cm⁻¹ is associated with defects and disorder.26 The intensity ratio,

ID​/IG​, serves as a powerful proxy for the degree of structural ordering and the size of the nanocrystalline graphitic domains within the amorphous matrix.27 For electrical and sensing applications, this information is paramount. Electrical conductivity in carbon materials is almost exclusively due to the presence of extended networks of sp²-hybridized carbon. Raman spectroscopy provides the most direct, non-destructive measurement of the formation and evolution of these graphitic structures. A rising

ID​/IG​ ratio with pyrolysis temperature is a direct indicator of the increasing carbonization and aromatization that underpins the material's transition from an insulator to a conductor. It provides the fundamental structural explanation for the electrical properties measured by other techniques.

### **3.3. Surface Chemistry and Elemental States (FTIR & XPS)**

While Raman spectroscopy probes the bulk carbon structure, Fourier-Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR) and X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) are surface-sensitive techniques that provide critical information about the chemical composition of the biochar.

FTIR operates by measuring the absorption of infrared radiation by the sample. Different chemical bonds and functional groups vibrate at characteristic frequencies, and when the frequency of the IR radiation matches the vibrational frequency of a specific bond, the radiation is absorbed. The resulting spectrum of absorbance versus frequency provides a qualitative "fingerprint" of the functional groups present on the biochar surface.3 For biochar, FTIR is excellent for identifying key groups such as hydroxyls (-OH), carbonyls (C=O), carboxyls (-COOH), and aromatic C=C bonds. Its primary role in this context is to track the removal of oxygen-containing groups—which are generally electrically insulating—as pyrolysis temperature increases. For sensor development, FTIR can also identify specific functional groups that could serve as active sites for binding target analytes or for further chemical modification.

XPS provides a more quantitative and detailed analysis of the surface chemistry. In XPS, the sample is irradiated with a beam of X-rays, causing the ejection of core-level electrons. The kinetic energy of these photoejected electrons is measured, and from this, their binding energy can be calculated. Each element has a unique set of core electron binding energies, allowing XPS to provide a quantitative elemental composition of the top few nanometers of the surface.3 More importantly, small shifts in an element's binding energy (chemical shifts) are caused by its local chemical environment. By acquiring high-resolution spectra of a specific element's peak (e.g., C 1s, O 1s) and deconvoluting it into its component peaks, XPS can identify and quantify the different chemical states of that element.2 For example, the C 1s spectrum can be resolved into components for graphitic C-C, C-O, C=O, and O-C=O bonds. This capability is indispensable for confirming the degree of carbonization (by measuring the C/O ratio) and for providing definitive, quantitative evidence of the formation of a conductive sp² carbon network.

### **3.4. Quantifying Porous Properties (BET Analysis)**

The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method is the standard technique for determining the specific surface area and porous properties of solid materials. The technique is based on the physical adsorption (physisorption) of an inert gas, typically nitrogen, onto the surface of the material at a constant cryogenic temperature (77 K, the boiling point of liquid nitrogen).30 A known amount of gas is introduced to the sample, and the amount of gas that adsorbs onto the surface is measured as a function of the relative pressure. The BET theory is then applied to the adsorption isotherm data to calculate the amount of gas required to form a single molecular layer (a monolayer) on the entire surface. From this value, the total specific surface area is calculated. Further analysis of the isotherm, particularly at higher relative pressures, can provide the total pore volume and the pore size distribution, which categorizes the pores into micropores (\< 2 nm), mesopores (2-50 nm), and macropores (\> 50 nm).30

For electrical and sensing applications, the information provided by BET analysis is absolutely essential. In supercapacitors, the capacitance is directly proportional to the electrochemically accessible surface area where the electric double-layer forms. A higher SSA translates directly to a higher capacity for energy storage.14 In electrochemical sensors, a high SSA maximizes the number of active sites available for interaction with the target analyte, which enhances the sensitivity and lowers the limit of detection of the sensor.17 The pore size distribution is also critical; mesopores are necessary to ensure rapid transport of electrolyte ions or analyte molecules into the interior of the porous structure, while micropores contribute significantly to the total surface area.

### **3.5. Thermal Stability and Compositional Integrity (TGA)**

Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA) is a thermal analysis technique that measures the change in mass of a sample as a function of temperature or time in a controlled atmosphere (e.g., inert nitrogen or oxidative air).31 The output is a thermogram, a plot of mass versus temperature, from which the derivative (DTG curve) can be calculated to show the rate of mass loss.35

TGA serves several critical functions in biochar characterization. Firstly, it determines the thermal stability of the final biochar product. A well-carbonized, high-temperature biochar should exhibit high thermal stability, showing minimal mass loss until very high temperatures are reached in an inert atmosphere, confirming that the initial pyrolysis process was effective and complete.31 Secondly, TGA can be used to perform a proximate analysis of the biochar. By running a programmed temperature sequence (e.g., heating in nitrogen to remove volatiles, then switching to air to combust the fixed carbon), TGA can accurately quantify the fractions of moisture, volatile matter, fixed carbon, and ash.37 The fixed carbon content is a crucial measure of the biochar's purity and represents the amount of electrochemically active material in an electrode. For any electrochemical device, knowing the precise mass of the active material is essential for normalizing performance metrics like specific capacitance (in F/g) or sensor response.

### **3.6. Intrinsic Electrical Property Measurement (Four-Probe Method)**

The four-probe method is the gold standard for measuring the electrical resistivity (and its inverse, conductivity) of semiconducting and conducting materials, especially those in thin film or bulk powder form where contact resistance can be a significant issue.32 The setup consists of four equally spaced, co-linear probes that are brought into contact with the material. A constant DC current (

I) is passed through the two outer probes, and the resulting voltage drop (V) is measured across the two inner probes.32

The primary advantage of this configuration is that it effectively eliminates the influence of contact resistance from the measurement. The voltmeter used to measure the voltage across the inner probes has a very high internal impedance, meaning negligible current flows through it. As a result, the voltage drop measured is due almost entirely to the intrinsic resistance of the material between the two inner probes, and is not affected by the resistance at the current-injecting contacts or the probe-to-material contacts of the voltage probes.32 From the measured current and voltage, the sheet resistance (

Rs​) can be calculated, and with the sample's dimensions (thickness and width), the bulk resistivity (ρ) and conductivity (σ=1/ρ) can be determined.38 For evaluating biochar for electrical applications, this technique is non-negotiable. It provides the most accurate, reliable, and reproducible measurement of the key performance metric—electrical conductivity. This allows for a direct, quantitative comparison of biochars produced under different conditions and provides the definitive link between the material's structure (probed by Raman) and its ultimate electronic function.

The synergy between these techniques forms a robust analytical workflow. TGA first confirms the basic composition and thermal integrity of the biochar. SEM then provides a visual confirmation of its physical structure. BET quantifies the surface area and porosity of that structure. FTIR and XPS define the chemical nature of the surface. Raman spectroscopy elucidates the internal arrangement of the carbon atoms. Finally, the four-probe measurement quantifies the resulting electrical property that arises from this specific combination of physical, chemical, and structural characteristics. This integrated approach enables a deep and comprehensive understanding of the material, facilitating the rational design of pecan shell biochar for high-performance applications.

## **Section 4: Synthesis and Recommendations for Application-Specific Biochar**

The comprehensive analysis of how pyrolysis temperature modulates the physicochemical properties of pecan shell biochar allows for the development of targeted synthesis strategies. The requirements for an ideal supercapacitor electrode are distinct from those of an optimal electrochemical sensor platform. By synthesizing the findings from the preceding sections, it is possible to prescribe specific pyrolysis regimes and post-processing steps to tailor pecan shell biochar for each of these advanced applications.

### **4.1. Designing Pecan Shell Biochar for Supercapacitor Electrodes**

The primary function of a supercapacitor electrode is to store charge efficiently at a high-surface-area interface with an electrolyte. The key performance metrics—specific capacitance, rate capability, and cycle life—are directly governed by a specific set of material properties. The ideal biochar for this application must possess:

1. **Maximized Specific Surface Area (SSA):** Capacitance in an electric double-layer capacitor (EDLC) is directly proportional to the SSA accessible to electrolyte ions. Therefore, the goal is to achieve the highest possible surface area.  
2. **Hierarchical Porosity:** An optimal pore structure consists of micropores to maximize the surface area and charge storage density, and mesopores to serve as ion-buffering reservoirs and highways, facilitating rapid ion transport to the microporous surfaces. This hierarchical structure is essential for achieving high power density and rate capability.  
3. **High Electrical Conductivity:** The carbon matrix itself must be highly conductive to ensure efficient charge collection and transport from the entire electrode volume to the current collector with minimal ohmic losses (IR drop).

Based on the established temperature-property relationships, the synthesis strategy for a high-performance supercapacitor electrode from pecan shells should involve high-temperature processing. Pyrolysis temperatures in the range of 800-1050°C are recommended. This high-temperature regime ensures two critical outcomes simultaneously: the development of a highly porous structure with a large SSA (e.g., 450 m²/g at 800°C) and the formation of a highly conductive, graphitized carbon framework.2

However, pyrolysis alone is often insufficient to reach the ultra-high surface areas (\>1000 m²/g) characteristic of commercial activated carbons. Therefore, an activation step is almost certainly required. This can be achieved through physical activation (e.g., with steam or CO₂ at high temperatures) or chemical activation (e.g., impregnation with agents like KOH or H₃PO₄ followed by heating).8 An exceptionally promising approach is "self-activation," where the in-situ gases generated during very high-temperature pyrolysis (e.g., 1050°C) act as the activating agents.14 This method has been demonstrated to produce pecan shell-derived activated carbon (PSAC) with an outstanding SSA of 1554 m²/g and an ordered, interconnected pore structure. The resulting supercapacitor electrodes exhibited a high specific capacitance of 269 F/g, excellent cycling stability over 15,000 cycles, and high energy and power densities, confirming that high-temperature, activated pecan shell biochar is a premier candidate for energy storage applications.14

### **4.2. Tailoring Pecan Shell Biochar for Electrochemical Sensor Platforms**

The material requirements for an electrochemical sensor platform are more nuanced and present a classic example of a materials engineering trade-off. An effective sensor electrode requires:

1. **Good Electrical Conductivity:** Similar to supercapacitors, the electrode must efficiently transduce the chemical recognition event into a measurable electrical signal. This favors a highly graphitized carbon structure produced at high temperatures.  
2. **High Specific Surface Area:** A large surface area is needed to maximize the loading of active sites, thereby enhancing the sensor's sensitivity and lowering its detection limit.  
3. **Specific Surface Functionality:** Unlike supercapacitors, where a pure carbon surface is often ideal, sensors may require specific surface functional groups (e.g., \-COOH, \-OH). These groups can act as direct recognition sites for certain analytes (e.g., heavy metals), improve the wettability and biocompatibility of the electrode, or serve as covalent anchoring points for immobilizing biorecognition elements like enzymes (for biosensors) or antibodies.16

This creates a conflict. The high pyrolysis temperatures needed to achieve good conductivity simultaneously eliminate the very surface functional groups that may be required for sensing activity.11 Producing biochar at a low temperature would preserve functional groups but result in an insulating material unsuitable for an electrode.

Therefore, a multi-step, tailored approach is recommended for producing sensor-grade biochar from pecan shells. The optimal strategy would be to first prioritize conductivity by pyrolyzing the pecan shells at a high temperature (e.g., \> 800°C) to create a conductive and high-surface-area carbon scaffold. Following this, a controlled post-processing surface modification step can be employed to re-introduce the desired functionality without compromising the bulk conductivity. Common methods for this include mild chemical oxidation using agents like nitric acid (HNO₃) or hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂), or electrochemical activation. This two-step process—high-temperature pyrolysis for the conductive framework followed by controlled surface functionalization—allows for the decoupling of these competing requirements. This approach would yield a material that possesses both the efficient electron transfer capabilities of high-temperature biochar and the selective chemical reactivity of a functionalized surface, making it an ideal platform for developing a wide range of sensitive and selective electrochemical sensors for detecting environmental pollutants, biomolecules, and pharmaceuticals.19

## **Section 5: Concluding Remarks and Future Outlook**

### **5.1. Summary of Key Findings**

This comprehensive analysis has systematically elucidated the profound impact of pyrolysis temperature on the transformation of pecan shell waste into a versatile, high-performance carbon material. The key findings establish a clear set of structure-property-processing relationships that are essential for the rational design of biochar for advanced applications. It is unequivocally demonstrated that increasing pyrolysis temperature leads to a predictable trade-off between biochar yield and carbon quality. Higher temperatures decrease the solid yield but significantly enhance critical properties for electrical and sensing applications, including:

* **Increased Porosity and Specific Surface Area:** The volatilization of biomass components at elevated temperatures creates a well-developed porous network, with specific surface areas reaching over 450 m²/g at 800°C.  
* **Enhanced Aromaticity and Carbonization:** The biochar surface transitions from a polar, functional-group-rich state to a nonpolar, highly aromatic state, as confirmed by the reduction of oxygen content and the dominance of sp²-hybridized carbon.  
* **Improved Structural Order:** The carbon matrix rearranges to form larger, more ordered nanocrystalline graphitic domains, as evidenced by an increasing Raman ID​/IG​ ratio.  
* **Dramatically Increased Electrical Conductivity:** The synergistic effect of forming an interconnected sp² carbon network and removing insulating functional groups results in a transition from an insulating material at low temperatures to a conductive one at high temperatures.

### **5.2. The Importance of Integrated Characterization**

A central conclusion of this report is that the development and optimization of functional biochar materials are critically dependent on an integrated, multi-technique characterization approach. No single analytical method can provide a complete picture. The true understanding of the material emerges from the logical synthesis of complementary data. The workflow proceeding from TGA (composition), SEM (morphology), and BET (porosity) to FTIR/XPS (surface chemistry) and Raman spectroscopy (internal structure), culminating in four-probe measurements (functional property), represents a powerful causal chain of analysis. This integrated methodology allows researchers to connect specific processing parameters directly to atomic-level structural changes and, ultimately, to the macroscopic performance of the material in a device. Adopting such a comprehensive characterization strategy is non-negotiable for advancing the field beyond empirical studies and toward predictive materials design.

### **5.3. Future Research Directions**

While the fundamental relationships are now well-established, several promising avenues for future research can further unlock the potential of pecan shell-derived carbon materials. The following directions are recommended:

* **Systematic Activation Studies:** While the benefits of activation are clear, there is a need for systematic studies that directly compare different activation methods (e.g., KOH, H₃PO₄, steam, CO₂) applied to pecan shell biochar produced at various initial pyrolysis temperatures. The goal should be to map the resulting porosity and surface chemistry to optimize materials for specific targets, such as the hierarchical pore structure needed for high-rate supercapacitors.8  
* **Heteroatom Doping:** The intentional introduction of heteroatoms like nitrogen, boron, or phosphorus into the carbon lattice (doping) is a proven strategy for enhancing the electrochemical performance of carbon materials. Doping can improve conductivity, create catalytically active sites, and enhance surface wettability. Future work should explore methods for doping pecan shell biochar, either during pyrolysis (by co-pyrolyzing with a nitrogen-rich precursor) or via post-pyrolysis treatments, and evaluate its performance in applications like the oxygen reduction reaction or advanced sensing.16  
* **Device Prototyping and Validation:** The majority of research has focused on material-level characterization. A crucial next step is to move towards the fabrication and rigorous testing of prototype devices. This includes assembling and testing full supercapacitor cells to evaluate their real-world energy density, power density, and long-term stability, and constructing modified electrodes for the selective and sensitive detection of high-impact analytes like glucose, dopamine, heavy metal ions, or specific pesticides.19  
* **Techno-Economic and Life Cycle Analysis:** To bridge the gap between laboratory-scale research and industrial viability, comprehensive techno-economic analyses are required. These studies should assess the costs associated with feedstock collection, pyrolysis, activation, and material processing to determine the economic feasibility of producing these advanced carbons at scale. Concurrently, a full life cycle assessment will quantify the net environmental benefits, including carbon sequestration potential and the impact of displacing more energy-intensive conventional materials, ensuring the overall sustainability of the valorization pathway.
