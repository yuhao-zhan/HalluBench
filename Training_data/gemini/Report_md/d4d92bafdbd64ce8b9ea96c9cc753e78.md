

# **Feasibility Analysis of a Simulated Natural Light Program for Broiler Chickens from Day-of-Hatch**

## **Executive Summary**

This report provides a comprehensive feasibility analysis of implementing a dynamic lighting system for commercial broiler chickens that simulates natural daylight cycles, commencing from the day of hatch. The proposed program consists of a 16-hour light period (16L), a 6-hour dark period (6D), and 1-hour transitional periods of gradually intensifying light (dawn) and gradually dimming light (dusk). The analysis evaluates the biological, production, technical, and economic feasibility of this advanced environmental management strategy.

Based on an exhaustive review of scientific literature and industry data, this report concludes that the implementation of such a program is not only **feasible but also highly recommended** for modern broiler production seeking to optimize welfare, performance, and sustainability.

The key findings supporting this conclusion are:

* **Biological & Welfare Feasibility:** The program aligns closely with the innate biological needs of broiler chickens. Providing a structured dark period from day-of-hatch establishes a robust circadian rhythm, evidenced by normalized melatonin secretion, without compromising end-of-flock performance. The gradual dawn and dusk transitions significantly reduce stress, as indicated by behavioral observations and physiological markers like corticosterone. This leads to calmer flock behavior, improved sleep quality, better skeletal and ocular health, and a reduction in welfare-related mortality.  
* **Production Feasibility:** The program demonstrates a positive or, at minimum, neutral impact on key production indicators. While initial first-week growth may be slightly slower compared to continuous lighting, birds exhibit compensatory growth, resulting in equivalent or improved final body weights. More significantly, the welfare benefits translate into tangible production gains, including improved feed conversion ratios (FCR) and lower overall mortality and culling rates. The behavioral synchronization fostered by the program also contributes to better flock uniformity.  
* **Technical & Economic Feasibility:** The technology required to execute this program—specifically programmable, flicker-free LED systems with precise, deep-dimming capabilities (to 0.1% or lower)—is commercially available from specialized agricultural lighting suppliers. While the initial capital expenditure for these systems is higher than for conventional lighting, the economic case is compelling. The return on investment is driven by substantial operational savings (up to 85% reduction in lighting-related energy costs and lower maintenance) and revenue enhancements from improved FCR and livability. The projected payback period for such an investment is typically under two years.

In conclusion, adopting a biomimetic lighting program represents a strategic evolution from viewing light as simple illumination to leveraging it as a powerful management tool. Success is contingent on investing in the correct high-precision technology. For producers and integrators aiming to enhance animal welfare, increase production efficiency, and improve sustainability, this lighting strategy offers a significant competitive advantage.

## **1.0 Introduction: The Evolution of Environmental Management in Poultry Production**

### **1.1 Beyond Illumination: The Scientific Understanding of Light's Role in Broiler Physiology, Neuroendocrinology, and Circadian Rhythms**

For decades, lighting in commercial poultry production was treated as a rudimentary utility, primarily to provide sufficient illumination for birds to see and to extend feeding times. However, a growing body of scientific research has fundamentally shifted this perspective. Light is now understood to be one of the most potent environmental signals influencing broiler chicken biology, acting as a critical regulator of physiology, neuroendocrinology, and behavior.1

Unlike mammals, light penetrates the avian skull and directly stimulates extra-retinal photoreceptors in the pineal gland and the hypothalamus, which houses the pituitary gland.3 This direct neural stimulation means that light profoundly influences the entire neuroendocrine system, regulating the secretion of key hormones responsible for growth, metabolism, immune function, and stress response.3

At the core of this regulation is the circadian rhythm, the bird's internal 24-hour clock. The ancestors of domestic chickens, the Red Junglefowl, are diurnal animals whose physiology and behavior are intrinsically synchronized to the natural cycle of day and night.6 This rhythm dictates patterns of feeding, activity, sleep, and hormone production. Modern intensive production practices, particularly the historical use of continuous (24L:0D) or near-continuous (23L:1D) lighting, have often been implemented with the goal of maximizing feed intake.6 However, this practice effectively disrupts the bird's innate biological clock, leading to a host of documented welfare and health challenges, including increased susceptibility to metabolic diseases like ascites, skeletal disorders such as tibial dyschondroplasia, elevated physiological stress, and impaired immune function.6

The technological evolution from inefficient incandescent bulbs to modern Light-Emitting Diode (LED) systems has been a game-changer. LEDs offer unprecedented, precise control over the three primary components of light: intensity (brightness), photoperiod (duration), and spectrum (color).2 This technological leap makes it possible, for the first time, to design and implement sophisticated, biomimetic lighting programs that more closely replicate the natural environmental cues poultry evolved with.

### **1.2 The Proposed Program: Deconstructing the 16L:6D Photoperiod with 1-Hour Dawn/Dusk Transitions**

This report assesses the feasibility of a specific biomimetic lighting program designed to leverage the scientific understanding of light's biological role. The proposed program is defined by the following parameters:

* **Photoperiod:** A daily cycle comprising 16 hours of light (photophase) and 8 hours of darkness/transition (scotophase).  
* **Dark Period:** A minimum of 6 continuous hours of complete darkness (defined as \<1 lux).  
* **Gradual Transitions:** A 1-hour period of gradually increasing light intensity to simulate dawn, and a 1-hour period of gradually decreasing light intensity to simulate dusk.

This structure results in a daily schedule of 1-hour dawn, 14 hours of full light, 1-hour dusk, and 8 hours of darkness. This 16L:8D schedule, with its substantial dark period and gradual transitions, is designed to provide a predictable, low-stress environment that supports the bird's natural circadian rhythm.7 This contrasts sharply with abrupt on/off systems and near-continuous lighting schedules.

## **2.0 Biological and Welfare Feasibility: The Impact on Bird Health and Behavior**

### **2.1 Entraining the Circadian System from Day-of-Hatch**

A foundational element of the proposed program is the introduction of a significant dark period starting from the day of hatch. This practice directly challenges the long-held industry convention of providing 23 or 24 hours of light during the first week of brooding.6 The traditional rationale was to maximize the opportunity for newly hatched chicks, which have immature digestive and thermoregulatory systems, to find feed and water.10

However, a robust body of recent scientific evidence refutes the necessity of this practice and highlights its detrimental effects. Research from multiple institutions has conclusively demonstrated that providing a dark period of 4 to 8 hours daily, starting from day one, does not negatively impact end-of-flock performance.21 While chicks on this schedule may exhibit slightly lower body weights during the first week, they undergo a period of compensatory growth and achieve equivalent or better final body weights and FCR compared to chicks brooded under continuous light.21

The primary biological benefit of introducing darkness early is the proper entrainment of the circadian system. The daily cycle of light and dark is the most powerful synchronizing agent for the bird's internal clock. Darkness is required for the pineal gland to produce and secrete the hormone melatonin.5 Studies show that chicks provided with a dark period from placement have significantly higher baseline and nighttime melatonin levels, a physiological advantage that persists throughout the entire grow-out period.6 This is critically important, as melatonin is not just a "sleep hormone"; it is a master regulator essential for coordinating sleep-wake cycles, bolstering immune competence, managing metabolic processes, and mediating stress responses.5 Continuous light suppresses melatonin production, disrupting these vital functions and leaving the bird in a state of physiological disarray.6

The first week of a broiler's life is a period of phenomenal physiological transition, with dramatic development of the digestive, immune, and skeletal systems.20 Establishing a stable, robust circadian rhythm during this critical window by providing a predictable dark period creates a more physiologically sound and resilient bird, better prepared to cope with the metabolic demands of rapid growth.

### **2.2 The Science of Gradual Transitions: Dawn and Dusk Simulation**

The inclusion of 1-hour gradual dawn and dusk periods is a cornerstone of the proposed program's welfare benefits. These transitions transform the abrupt, stressful changes of conventional on/off lighting into a predictable and calming environmental cue.

Behavioral studies, particularly on laying hens, provide clear evidence of the benefits. A simulated dusk prompts birds to engage in natural pre-roosting behaviors.24 Activity gradually decreases as birds anticipate the dark period, with a final peak in feeding and drinking to "fuel up" for the night.6 This allows the flock to move towards resting areas and settle calmly. When full darkness arrives, a significant portion of the flock is already sitting and resting, in stark contrast to the startle response, panic, and potential for injurious piling that can occur when lights are extinguished suddenly.24 Similarly, a simulated dawn facilitates a gradual, calm awakening. Activity increases slowly and in a synchronized manner, preventing the sharp, chaotic spike in movement seen with abrupt "lights-on" systems.24

These behavioral responses are mirrored by physiological indicators of stress. Direct research on quail, another gallinaceous bird, demonstrated that a sunrise/sunset lighting system resulted in numerically lower concentrations of corticosterone—the primary avian stress hormone—compared to sudden on/off lighting.25 While some studies on intermittent lighting show mixed effects on corticosterone 27, this is a different type of program; the key benefit of dawn/dusk is its predictability, which reduces the stress associated with sudden environmental change. Further supporting this, studies on heat-stressed broilers found that lighting programs providing dedicated rest periods significantly relieved stress biomarkers, including corticosterone.28

The ultimate welfare benefit of these calm transitions is improved sleep. While direct electroencephalogram (EEG) analysis of broiler sleep under dawn/dusk simulation is an emerging field, strong inferences can be drawn from existing research. Sleep is vital for restoration, growth, and cognitive function, and achieving the necessary amounts of both deep sleep (slow-wave sleep, SWS) and REM sleep requires periods of undisturbed rest.29 Gradual transitions are critical because they prevent disturbances at the very moments birds are trying to initiate or end their primary rest period.29 A recent study confirmed that broilers in a gradual transition system exhibited more sitting and sleeping behaviors.32 By creating a predictable and calm transition to darkness, the proposed system facilitates longer, higher-quality, and more synchronized resting bouts throughout the flock, which is a powerful proxy for improved sleep quality and, therefore, enhanced welfare.31

### **2.3 Holistic Health and Welfare Outcomes**

The cumulative effects of a properly entrained circadian rhythm and reduced daily stress manifest in several key health and welfare improvements.

**Skeletal Health:** One of the most consistent findings in poultry lighting research is the link between photoperiod and leg health. Long daylengths are strongly associated with a higher incidence of skeletal disorders, such as tibial dyschondroplasia and valgus-varus deformation, and consequently, poorer gait scores.6 Conversely, providing an adequate dark period of at least 6-8 hours allows for more natural bone development and has been shown to decrease lameness and the incidence of leg abnormalities, thereby reducing a major cause of culling and mortality.6 The proposed 16L:8D schedule is therefore highly conducive to improved skeletal integrity.

**Ocular Health:** Vision is a dominant sense for poultry, and their ocular health is sensitive to lighting conditions. Rearing broilers under continuous or near-continuous dim lighting has been implicated in the development of eye abnormalities, including avian glaucoma and macrophthalmos (enlarged eyes).8 Providing a distinct dark period is essential for normal ocular development and function. While extremely dim light (\<5 lux) can also be detrimental, a well-managed program that provides adequate brightness during the photophase and true darkness during the scotophase mitigates these risks.8

**Behavioral Repertoire:** A lighting program that provides both temporal variation (light/dark) and the opportunity for choice promotes a wider range of natural behaviors, which is a key indicator of positive welfare. Birds on shorter photoperiods exhibit more comfort behaviors (preening) and exploratory behaviors (foraging, dustbathing).6 Enriched lighting programs, which share the principle of providing environmental complexity, have been shown to stimulate voluntary walking and dustbathing, further improving leg health and mental well-being.34 The proposed program, by creating distinct periods for activity and rest, encourages a more natural and diverse behavioral repertoire compared to monotonous, continuous-light environments.

## **3.0 Production and Economic Feasibility: From Barn Floor to Bottom Line**

### **3.1 Impact on Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**

The welfare benefits derived from a biomimetic lighting program translate directly into measurable production and economic outcomes. While the relationship between lighting and performance can be complex, the evidence points toward a net positive impact.

**Feed Conversion Ratio (FCR) and Body Weight (BW):** The data on FCR and BW under various lighting schemes requires nuanced interpretation. While some older studies or those using suboptimal dawn/dusk simulations reported no significant differences in performance 35, a compelling body of modern research indicates that improved welfare leads to improved efficiency. Studies on enriched and variable lighting systems—which, like the proposed program, reduce stress and provide environmental choice—demonstrate improved daily weight gain and, crucially, better FCR.34 Research has also shown that lower light intensities can improve FCR.22 One study using LED lighting found a more favorable FCR in all weeks compared to incandescent lighting.38 The likely mechanisms for this improvement are twofold: first, reduced energy expenditure due to lower stress and hyperactivity, and second, more efficient nutrient utilization resulting from rhythmic, anticipatory feeding patterns.14 As noted, while birds on an early-darkness program may have lower first-week weights, they exhibit compensatory growth and match or exceed their counterparts by the end of the flock.21 Therefore, the proposed program is highly likely to result in a net improvement in FCR and achieve target body weights.

**Mortality, Culling, and Uniformity:** The link between the proposed photoperiod and flock livability is unequivocal. Long daylengths are directly and linearly correlated with higher mortality, particularly from metabolic issues (e.g., ascites, sudden death syndrome) and skeletal problems.6 Providing at least 6-8 hours of darkness is one of the most effective management tools for reducing these sources of mortality.14 Furthermore, lighting programs that reduce stress and encourage natural movement, such as enriched lighting, lead to a lower number of birds culled due to leg problems.34 The behavioral synchronization driven by dawn and dusk transitions, which ensures the entire flock is on a similar schedule for feeding, drinking, and resting, is also likely to improve overall flock uniformity. This was demonstrated in a study where a broiler-specific light spectrum significantly improved the uniformity index.1

### **3.2 Technical System Requirements for Implementation**

The successful implementation of the proposed program is entirely dependent on the use of appropriate, modern lighting technology. It is not feasible with legacy systems like incandescent or compact fluorescent (CFL) bulbs.

**Poultry-Specific Programmable LED Systems:** The core requirement is a high-quality LED system that is specifically designed for agricultural environments and can be integrated with an environmental controller.2 These systems must be robust enough to withstand the harsh conditions of a poultry house, including high-pressure washing and ammonia exposure, necessitating a high Ingress Protection (IP) rating (e.g., IP67 or IP69).16

**The Criticality of Dimming Precision:** This is the single most important technical specification and is non-negotiable for achieving the welfare benefits of gradual transitions. Standard residential or industrial dimmers, which often have a minimum dimming level of 10% or 5%, are wholly inadequate. From a bird's perspective, a sudden drop from 5% intensity to 0% is still an abrupt, stressful event.16 A true, gradual dawn/dusk simulation that mimics nature requires a system capable of dimming smoothly and without any perceptible flicker from 100% down to 1%, 0.1%, or even 0.01%.12 This deep-dimming capability is a key feature that distinguishes premium, poultry-specific lighting systems from cheaper alternatives.

**Spectrum Control:** To fully replicate a natural sunrise and sunset, the lighting system should ideally be able to shift its color spectrum. Natural dawn and dusk are characterized by light rich in longer, red wavelengths, while midday light is cooler and richer in blue wavelengths.13 Research has shown that broilers exhibit a preference for warmer, redder-hued light (e.g., 2700K) during the first and last hours of the day.2 While not strictly essential for feasibility, incorporating spectral control through tunable-white or multi-channel (RGB) LEDs represents the next frontier of optimization. Broiler-specific light spectrums, which are fixed combinations of blue, green, and red LEDs, have already been shown to significantly improve body weight and flock uniformity.1

### **3.3 Economic Analysis and Return on Investment (ROI)**

The adoption of an advanced lighting system represents a significant capital investment, but one that offers a clear and often rapid return through a combination of reduced operational costs and enhanced production revenue.

**Capital Expenditure (CAPEX):** The initial investment for a poultry-specific, deep-dimming LED system is higher than for basic LEDs or traditional lighting. Costs can range from approximately $7,000 to $11,000 USD (£5,500 to £9,000) to outfit a typical commercial broiler house, depending on the system's sophistication and features.12

**Operational Expenditure (OPEX) Savings:** This is the most direct and easily quantifiable component of the ROI.

* **Energy Savings:** LED technology is vastly more efficient than legacy sources, offering energy savings of 80-85% compared to incandescent bulbs and substantial savings over CFLs.11 Real-world case studies have documented electricity cost reductions of 67% 43 and annual savings of over $1,000 (£867) per house just from switching from fluorescent to LED systems.41  
* **Maintenance and Replacement Savings:** The long operational lifespan of high-quality LEDs (e.g., 60,000+ hours) compared to incandescent (2,000-5,000 hours) or CFL (10,000 hours) bulbs drastically reduces the cost of replacement bulbs and the labor required for their installation.16

**Revenue Enhancement and Cost Reduction:** These returns are derived from the production benefits outlined previously.

* **Improved FCR:** Even a marginal improvement in FCR translates into significant feed cost savings. For a house producing 150,000 kg of live weight per year, a 2-point (0.02) improvement in FCR at a feed cost of $450/ton would save approximately $1,350 annually. Enriched lighting programs have demonstrated such improvements.34  
* **Reduced Mortality:** A 1% reduction in mortality in a 25,000-bird house, with a final bird value of $5, means an additional $1,250 in revenue per flock. The proposed program is strongly linked to reduced mortality from metabolic and skeletal issues.14  
* **Improved Grade and Yield:** Better flock uniformity can lead to a higher percentage of birds meeting Grade A specifications, reducing downgrades at the processing plant and potentially improving overall meat yield.1

## **4.0 Synthesis and Actionable Recommendations**

### **4.1 Integrated Feasibility Verdict**

The comprehensive analysis across biological, production, technical, and economic domains leads to an unambiguous conclusion: the implementation of a 16L:8D lighting program with 1-hour gradual dawn and dusk transitions, starting from the day of hatch, is not only feasible but represents a significant advancement in broiler production management. The biological and welfare benefits are substantial and well-documented. These welfare improvements are intrinsically linked to positive or neutral production outcomes, with a high probability of net economic gain through superior FCR and flock livability. The necessary technology is commercially available from specialized suppliers, and the return on investment is compelling, with projected payback periods of less than two years. This program is a scientifically sound, welfare-positive, and economically viable strategy.

### **4.2 A Phased Protocol for Implementation**

For producers seeking to adopt this program, a phased approach based on the age of the birds is recommended to maximize benefits.

**Brooding Phase (Days 1-7):**

* **Photoperiod:** Implement the full 16L:8D schedule, including the 1-hour dawn and dusk transitions, immediately from the day of chick placement. Do not use a 23L:1D or continuous light schedule.  
* **Intensity:** Provide a relatively bright and uniform light intensity of **20 to 40 lux** at bird level during the 14 hours of full light.8 This higher intensity is crucial during the first week to encourage activity and ensure all chicks quickly and easily locate feed and water sources.  
* **Spectrum:** If the system allows for spectral tuning, a warmer color temperature (e.g., 2700K \- 3000K) is preferable, as it is less stressful and may improve uniformity.16 If not, a standard dimmable white LED is sufficient.

**Grow-Out Phase (Day 8 onwards):**

* **Intensity Reduction:** After day 7, gradually reduce the light intensity during the main 14-hour photophase to a dimmer level, typically **5 to 10 lux**.8 This reduction conserves significant energy, helps keep the flock calm, improves FCR by reducing activity-related energy expenditure, and can mitigate aggressive behaviors like scratching and feather pecking.8  
* **Integrating Temporal and Spatial Variation (Advanced Recommendation):** To achieve the highest level of welfare and performance, it is strongly recommended to combine the *temporal* variation of the dawn/dusk cycle with *spatial* variation in light intensity during the day. This "enriched lighting" or "gradient lighting" approach involves using feedline-mounted lights or targeted programming to provide brighter zones (**\>20 lux**) directly over feeders and drinkers, while allowing other areas of the house (e.g., near the walls) to remain dimmer (**\<5 lux**).2 This strategy leverages the bird's natural preference to eat and drink in bright areas while seeking out dimmer areas for rest and comfort behaviors.2 This combination of temporal and spatial dynamics represents the pinnacle of current lighting management, allowing birds to choose their preferred environment based on their immediate behavioral needs, thereby maximizing the benefits of the lighting program.

### **4.3 Risk Management and Performance Monitoring**

The transition to any new management system requires careful monitoring and risk mitigation.

* **Key Metrics to Track:** Producers should establish baseline data for their current lighting program and meticulously track KPIs after implementation. Key metrics include FCR, final BW, mortality (total and categorized by cause, e.g., metabolic, skeletal), culling rates, flock uniformity at various ages, and welfare indicators like footpad dermatitis and hock burn scores.  
* **System Integrity:** A lighting system is only as good as its operational consistency. Regular visual inspections are necessary to ensure all lamps are functioning correctly and that dimmers are operating smoothly without flicker. Light meters should be used periodically to verify that programmed light intensities are being delivered accurately and uniformly (or in the intended gradient) at bird level. A single malfunctioning dimmer or a cluster of dead bulbs can create unintended dark spots that disrupt bird distribution and behavior.4  
* **Emergency Preparedness:** As with any critical life-support system in a poultry house, the lighting and control systems must be connected to a reliable emergency power source. Functional alarm systems that alert personnel to power or system failures are mandatory to prevent catastrophic losses and ensure bird welfare is maintained at all times.46

## **5.0 Conclusion: The Competitive Advantage of Biomimetic Lighting**

The evidence presented in this report marks a paradigm shift in the understanding and application of light in broiler production. Moving beyond the simplistic model of light as mere illumination, the industry now has the scientific foundation and technological capability to employ light as a sophisticated, non-invasive management tool.

The proposed program—a 16L:8D photoperiod with gradual dawn and dusk transitions initiated from hatch—is a prime example of this evolution. By designing an environment that respects and leverages the bird's innate biology, a practice known as biomimicry, producers can unlock a powerful "triple win":

1. **Improved Animal Welfare:** The program directly addresses key welfare concerns by reducing stress, enabling proper sleep, promoting natural behaviors, and improving skeletal health. This not only fulfills an ethical obligation but also meets the rising expectations of consumers and regulatory bodies for more humane animal husbandry.  
2. **Enhanced Production Efficiency:** The welfare benefits are not achieved at the expense of performance. To the contrary, a less-stressed, healthier, and more physiologically balanced bird is a more efficient bird. The program leads to tangible economic gains through improved feed conversion, lower mortality, and better flock uniformity.  
3. **Greater Sustainability:** The use of high-efficiency, long-lasting LED technology, combined with dimmer light levels during the grow-out phase, dramatically reduces a farm's energy consumption and carbon footprint, contributing to a more sustainable and cost-effective operation.

In an increasingly competitive global market where animal welfare standards, production efficiency, and environmental sustainability are becoming key differentiators, the adoption of advanced, biomimetic lighting is not a superfluous expense. It is a strategic investment that yields a clear competitive advantage, paving the way for a more productive, profitable, and responsible future for the poultry industry.
